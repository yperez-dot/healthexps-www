Understanding the Key Differences Between MAPD and Traditional Medicare

Published May 25, 2025

When you turn 65, you have a choice: stick with Original Medicare (Parts A and B) or enroll in a Medicare Advantage plan (also called MAPD when it includes drug coverage).

Both cover hospital and medical care. Both are legitimate Medicare options. But they work completely differently — and the one that's right for you depends on how you use healthcare, where you live, and what you value most.

Here's a clear breakdown of the key differences.

What Is Traditional Medicare?

Traditional Medicare is the original federal program, run by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS). It has two parts:

  • Part A (Hospital Insurance): Covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing, hospice, and some home health. Free for most people.
  • Part B (Medical Insurance): Covers doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services, durable medical equipment. Standard premium in 2026 is $202.90/month.

With Traditional Medicare, you can see any doctor or specialist in the U.S. that accepts Medicare — which is about 95% of providers. No networks. No referrals. No prior authorizations (in most cases).

What Traditional Medicare doesn't cover:

  • Prescription drugs (you need a standalone Part D plan)
  • Dental, vision, or hearing
  • Out-of-pocket maximum (your costs can keep going up)

Most people on Traditional Medicare also buy a Medicare Supplement (Medigap) plan to cover copays, coinsurance, and deductibles. Medigap premiums in Florida typically range from $300–$375/month for Plan G.

What Is MAPD (Medicare Advantage with Part D)?

Medicare Advantage is a private insurance plan that replaces Traditional Medicare. These plans are sold by private carriers like Humana, UnitedHealthcare, Aetna, and others — but they still provide Medicare coverage.

MAPD stands for Medicare Advantage + Part D, meaning the plan bundles:

  • Part A (Hospital)
  • Part B (Medical)
  • Part D (Prescription Drugs)

Most Medicare Advantage plans also include extra benefits that Traditional Medicare doesn't cover:

  • Dental, vision, and hearing
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) allowance cards
  • Fitness memberships (gym, SilverSneakers, Renew Active)
  • Transportation to doctor appointments
  • Meal delivery after hospital stays
  • Telehealth

What's the catch? Medicare Advantage plans use networks. You must see doctors and specialists within the plan's network (unless it's a PPO with out-of-network coverage). And many plans require referrals and prior authorizations for specialist care and procedures.

Who Should Choose Medicare Advantage?

Medicare Advantage tends to work well for people who:

  • Use healthcare infrequently: Lower premiums matter more than provider freedom.
  • Want extra benefits: Dental, vision, hearing, gym memberships, OTC cards.
  • Don't mind networks: Comfortable staying in-network for care.
  • Want a $0 or low premium: Many Medicare Advantage plans have $0 monthly premiums.
  • Want an out-of-pocket maximum: Prefer knowing the worst-case scenario for annual costs.

Who Should Choose Traditional Medicare + Medigap?

Traditional Medicare + Medigap tends to work well for people who:

  • See specialists frequently: No referrals, no prior authorizations, no network restrictions.
  • Travel frequently: Full nationwide coverage (and some international emergency coverage with Medigap).
  • Want provider freedom: Can see any doctor or specialist that accepts Medicare.
  • Prefer predictable costs: Medigap covers most out-of-pocket expenses, so you know what you'll pay each month.
  • Have chronic conditions or complex care needs: Easier to coordinate care across multiple specialists without network restrictions.

Can You Switch Between Them?

Yes, but there are rules.

Switching from Traditional Medicare to Medicare Advantage: You can do this during the Annual Enrollment Period (October 15–December 7) or certain Special Enrollment Periods. No medical underwriting required.

Switching from Medicare Advantage back to Traditional Medicare: You can do this during AEP or the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (January 1–March 31). However, if you want to buy a Medigap plan, you may face medical underwriting unless you're within your Medigap Open Enrollment Period (first 6 months after enrolling in Part B at age 65 or older). After that window, insurers can deny you or charge more based on health conditions.

Important: You Can't Have Both

You cannot be enrolled in Traditional Medicare and Medicare Advantage at the same time. It's one or the other.

If you enroll in a Medicare Advantage plan, your Traditional Medicare coverage is suspended (but not canceled). If you disenroll from Medicare Advantage, your Traditional Medicare coverage automatically resumes.

FAQs

Can I have Medicare Advantage and Medigap at the same time?

No. It's illegal for an insurance company to sell you a Medigap plan if you're enrolled in Medicare Advantage. You must choose one or the other.

Do I need Part D if I have Medicare Advantage?

Most Medicare Advantage plans include Part D drug coverage (these are called MAPD plans). If your Medicare Advantage plan does not include drug coverage, you can enroll in a standalone Part D plan.

Can I switch from Medicare Advantage to Traditional Medicare anytime?

You can switch during the Annual Enrollment Period (October 15–December 7) or the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (January 1–March 31). Outside those windows, you'll need a Special Enrollment Period or qualifying event.

Which is better: Medicare Advantage or Traditional Medicare?

There's no universal "better" option. It depends on your doctors, healthcare usage, budget, and whether you value provider freedom or extra benefits more. We help you compare both options based on your situation.
