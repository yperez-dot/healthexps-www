Why is there a Medicare Premium? I thought it was free!

Published March 7, 2023

One of the most common surprises people encounter when they turn 65 is discovering that Medicare isn't entirely free. You worked for decades, paid into the system every paycheck, and now there's a monthly bill waiting for you. It feels wrong — but there's a reason for it, and once you understand the structure, it makes a lot more sense.

The Short Answer: Medicare Has Four Parts — and They Don't All Work the Same Way

Medicare is divided into separate programs, each with its own funding source, rules, and cost structure. Whether you pay a premium depends entirely on which part of Medicare you're talking about.

Part A — Hospital Insurance: Usually Free

Medicare Part A covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice, and some home health services. For most people, Part A has no monthly premium — and here's why: you already paid for it.

Throughout your working years, 1.45% of every paycheck was withheld for Medicare taxes (your employer matched that amount). If you or your spouse worked and paid Medicare taxes for at least 40 quarters — that's 10 years — you've earned premium-free Part A. The vast majority of people turning 65 meet this threshold.

If you didn't work long enough to earn premium-free Part A, you can still enroll — but you'll pay a monthly premium. In 2026, that premium can be up to $518/month depending on how many quarters you worked. This situation is uncommon but worth knowing about, especially for people who immigrated to the U.S. later in life or spent most of their career self-employed without paying into the system.

Part B — Medical Insurance: Yes, There Is a Premium

This is where the surprise usually hits. Medicare Part B covers doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services, lab tests, durable medical equipment, and more — essentially most of what you'd consider "regular" medical care outside a hospital. Unlike Part A, Part B is not pre-paid through payroll taxes. It's funded through a combination of general government revenue and the monthly premiums beneficiaries pay.

In 2026, the standard Part B premium is $202.90 per month. For a couple, that's over $400 a month just for basic Medicare coverage — before any copays, deductibles, or additional insurance. This is why so many people are caught off guard: they heard "Medicare" and assumed "free," but only Part A is typically premium-free.

Part D — Prescription Drug Coverage: Separate Premium

Medicare Part D helps cover the cost of prescription medications. This is provided through private insurance companies approved by Medicare, and each plan charges its own monthly premium. Premiums vary widely depending on the plan you choose and where you live. If you skip Part D when you're first eligible and enroll later, you may face a permanent late enrollment penalty added to your premium — so it pays to enroll on time even if you currently take no medications.

What Is IRMAA? (Higher Earners Pay More)

If your income is above a certain threshold, you'll pay more than the standard Part B and Part D premiums. This is called IRMAA — the Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount. It's an extra charge added to your premium based on your income from two years prior.

For 2026, IRMAA kicks in when your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) exceeds:

  • $109,000 for individuals filing single
  • $218,000 for married couples filing jointly

The surcharge scales up from there in income tiers. At the highest income bracket, beneficiaries can pay more than $500/month for Part B alone. If your income has recently dropped — due to retirement, the sale of a home, or a life-changing event — you can appeal your IRMAA determination with the Social Security Administration to have it adjusted to reflect your current income.

Is There Help Paying the Premiums?

Yes — and more people qualify than you might think. There are two key assistance programs:

Medicare Savings Programs (MSP)

These are state-administered programs that can help low- and moderate-income individuals pay their Medicare premiums, deductibles, and copays. The most common benefit is having your entire Part B premium paid on your behalf — that's $202.90/month back in your pocket. Eligibility is based on income and assets, and the limits are more generous than most people assume. You may qualify even if you own a home or have some savings.

Extra Help (Low Income Subsidy for Part D)

Extra Help is a federal program that reduces or eliminates Part D prescription drug costs for people with limited income and resources. Depending on your income level, it can significantly lower your monthly drug plan premium, your annual deductible, and your copays at the pharmacy. Many people who qualify for Extra Help are automatically enrolled, but if you haven't been notified, it's worth applying through Social Security.

What About Medicare Advantage? Is That Free?

Medicare Advantage (Part C) is an alternative way to receive your Medicare benefits through a private insurance company. Many Medicare Advantage plans advertise a $0 premium, which sounds appealing — but it's important to understand what that means (and doesn't mean).

Even with a $0-premium Medicare Advantage plan, you are still required to pay your Part B premium of $202.90/month. The $0 refers only to the plan's additional monthly charge on top of Part B. Additionally, Medicare Advantage plans often have their own copays, coinsurance, deductibles, and network restrictions that affect your total out-of-pocket costs. A plan with a $0 premium isn't necessarily cheaper than one with a small monthly premium — it depends on how often you use care.

Quick Summary: Medicare Costs at a Glance (2026)

  • Part A premium: $0 for most people (requires 40+ quarters of Medicare-taxed work)
  • Part B premium: $202.90/month (standard; higher if income exceeds ~$106K single/$212K joint)
  • Part D premium: Varies by plan; separate from Parts A and B
  • IRMAA surcharge: Applies to Part B and Part D for higher-income beneficiaries
  • Medicare Savings Programs: Can pay your Part B premium if you qualify
  • Extra Help: Can reduce Part D costs if you have limited income/resources

The Bottom Line

Medicare is not entirely free — but it's also not as expensive as private insurance for most people. Part A is usually premium-free because you paid into it through decades of work. Part B requires a monthly contribution because it's a benefit that goes beyond what payroll taxes funded. Understanding the difference helps you plan ahead, avoid surprises, and find assistance programs that may be available to you.
