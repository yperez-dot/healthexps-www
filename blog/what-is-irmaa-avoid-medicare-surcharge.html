What Is IRMAA — and How to Avoid the Medicare Surcharge

What Is IRMAA — and How to Avoid the Medicare Surcharge

You retire, you enroll in Medicare, and then a letter from Social Security arrives saying your Part B premium won't be the standard $202.90 a month — it'll be hundreds more. Welcome to IRMAA: the Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount, Medicare's surcharge for higher earners.

The part that catches almost everyone off guard: IRMAA is based on your income from two years ago. Your 2026 premiums are set by your 2024 tax return — often your last full year of working income. Retiring is precisely when people get hit hardest, and it's also when they have the strongest case to fight it.

How IRMAA works

Each fall, Social Security looks at the modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) from your tax return two years back. MAGI here means your adjusted gross income plus tax-exempt interest — yes, municipal bond interest counts. If it's above the threshold, you pay a surcharge on top of your Part B premium and a separate surcharge on your Part D drug coverage. Both members of a married couple pay it individually.

For 2026, the first IRMAA threshold is $109,000 for a single filer and $218,000 for a joint return, based on 2024 income.

$109,000 or less $218,000 or less $202.90 (standard)
Above $109,000 Above $218,000 ~$284

The most important thing to know: IRMAA is a cliff, not a slope

Cross a threshold by one dollar and you pay the entire tier's surcharge for the whole year. A $109,001 income and a $109,000 income can differ by well over a thousand dollars in annual Medicare premiums. This is why IRMAA planning is really income-timing planning.

How to fight an IRMAA decision: Form SSA-44

If your income has dropped since that two-year-old tax return because of a life-changing event, you don't have to accept the surcharge. File Form SSA-44 and Social Security can recalculate using your current, lower income. Qualifying events include:

  • Work stoppage or reduction — retirement is the big one
  • Marriage, divorce, or the death of a spouse
  • Loss of income-producing property (beyond your control)
  • Loss or reduction of a pension
  • An employer settlement payment

Retired in 2025 or 2026 but being charged based on your 2024 salary? That's the textbook SSA-44 case. We walk clients through this filing routinely — bring your letter to a free appointment and we'll look at it together.

One thing SSA-44 does not cover: a one-time income spike (a big capital gain, a large Roth conversion, a lump-sum withdrawal). Those aren't "life-changing events" — which is why the planning below matters.

Planning moves that keep you under the thresholds

We're insurance brokers, not tax advisors — run these with your CPA or financial advisor. But these are the levers they'll likely discuss:

  1. Time your Roth conversions before age 63. Since IRMAA looks back two years, income you generate at 63+ shapes your premiums at 65+. Large conversions are often best done earlier — and once converted, Roth withdrawals don't count toward MAGI at all.
  2. Watch one-time gains near a threshold. Selling a rental property, a business, or a big stock position in a single tax year can trigger two years of IRMAA. Spreading a sale across tax years can keep you under a cliff.
  3. Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs). After age 70½, giving to charity directly from an IRA satisfies required distributions without adding to your MAGI.
  4. Remember tax-exempt interest counts. Municipal bonds reduce your taxes, not your IRMAA exposure.
  5. Check both spouses' timing. IRMAA thresholds for joint filers aren't double the single thresholds at the top tiers, and both spouses on Medicare pay the surcharge — a household planning conversation, not an individual one.

IRMAA also changes which Medicare setup makes sense

If you're paying an IRMAA surcharge, your total premium picture shifts — and sometimes that changes the Medicare Advantage vs. Medicare Supplement math too. The surcharge applies either way (it's tied to Part B and Part D, not to which plan style you pick), but higher fixed costs make it worth re-running your total annual cost comparison. The bottom line

IRMAA isn't a penalty for doing something wrong — it's a means-tested premium with a two-year lag and sharp cliffs. Frequently Asked Questions

What income is IRMAA based on?
Your modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) — adjusted gross income plus tax-exempt interest — from your federal tax return two years prior. Your 2026 IRMAA is based on your 2024 return.

What is the IRMAA income limit for 2026?
The surcharge begins above $109,000 in 2024 MAGI for single filers and $218,000 for married couples filing jointly. Higher tiers apply at higher incomes, up to a top tier at $500,000/$750,000.

Can I appeal IRMAA?
Yes. If your income dropped due to a life-changing event such as retirement, marriage, divorce, or the death of a spouse, file Form SSA-44 and Social Security can recalculate your surcharge using your current income.

Does IRMAA apply to Medicare Advantage plans?
Yes. IRMAA is charged on your Part B premium and as a separate Part D amount regardless of whether you choose Original Medicare, a Medicare Supplement, or a Medicare Advantage plan.

Is IRMAA permanent?
No. It's recalculated every year based on the tax return from two years prior. If your income falls below the thresholds, the surcharge goes away in the corresponding year.
