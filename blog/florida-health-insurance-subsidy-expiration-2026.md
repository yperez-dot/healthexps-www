If your health insurance bill suddenly jumped this year and you're wondering what happened — you're not alone. Millions of Floridians are in the exact same situation right now, and the short answer is this: the enhanced subsidies that made Affordable Care Act (ACA) coverage affordable for so many people expired on December 31, 2025.

What that means in plain language? Premiums went up. ## What Were the Enhanced Subsidies — and Why Are They Gone?

Back in 2021, the American Rescue Plan expanded the ACA's premium tax credits (PTCs) — the subsidies that lower your monthly premium. These enhancements were extended through 2025 under the Inflation Reduction Act. They were a big deal: they made it so that many people paid little to nothing for a Silver plan, and even middle-income earners got meaningful savings.

Those enhancements expired December 31, 2025. Congress did not renew them.

Here's what that looks like in real numbers for a Florida family:

- A family of four earning $85,000/year might have paid **~$350/month** in 2025 with enhanced subsidies.
- In 2026, that same family could be looking at **$550–$700/month** — or more — for the same plan.

That's not a small change. That's hundreds of dollars a month that people weren't budgeting for. If you're searching for "health insurance after subsidy expiration 2026" trying to make sense of this, the confusion is completely understandable.

## Your Real Options Right Now

The good news: you have more choices than you might think. The key is understanding what each option actually costs and covers — which is where a licensed agent makes all the difference.

### 1. Stay on Your ACA Marketplace Plan (With or Without Remaining Subsidies)

Even without the enhanced credits, you may still qualify for some baseline premium tax credits depending on your income. The original ACA subsidies are still in place — it's the *enhanced* portion that expired.

Log into **HealthCare.gov** and run a fresh quote with your current income. If your situation changed (you earn less, you have more dependents, your household changed), you might qualify for more help than you expect under the base ACA rules.

**Florida health insurance 2026** pricing varies significantly by county, carrier, and plan metal tier. Don't assume your 2025 plan is still your best value — do a fresh comparison.

### 2. Look at Private (Off-Marketplace) Health Insurance

Not every health plan is sold on the ACA marketplace. There are private plans — sometimes called "off-exchange" plans — that don't run through HealthCare.gov. These can sometimes offer lower premiums, though they work differently from ACA plans.

**Important:** Off-exchange private plans have tradeoffs. They may not cover pre-existing conditions the same way, and you won't get ACA tax credits on them. But for a healthy 30-year-old in Miami who earns too much for subsidies, a private plan might be worth exploring as part of an **affordable health insurance Miami** search.

This is the kind of comparison where having a licensed agent in your corner — someone who can actually run the numbers side by side — saves you from making a costly mistake.

### 3. COBRA: A Bridge, Not a Long-Term Solution

If you recently lost job-based coverage, COBRA lets you keep your employer's plan temporarily — typically up to 18 months. But here's the catch: **you pay the full premium**, including the portion your employer used to cover. For most people, COBRA is expensive.

When does COBRA make sense? If you're in the middle of an ongoing medical situation and changing networks or plans mid-treatment feels risky. Or if you expect your gap in coverage to be short. For most Florida families looking for affordable health insurance without Obamacare subsidies, COBRA is usually a stepping stone rather than a destination.

### 4. Medicaid and CHIP (If Your Income Qualifies)

Florida expanded Medicaid in 2023, which opened the door for more low-income adults to qualify. If your income dropped this year — or if you have children in the household — it's worth checking whether your family qualifies for Medicaid or the Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP).

Eligibility is based on current income, so even if you didn't qualify before, a job change or reduced hours might change your status. A licensed agent can help you check this at no cost to you.

## What About "Health Insurance Without Obamacare Florida"?

We hear this a lot. People are frustrated and looking for a way out of the marketplace entirely. There are a few categories worth knowing about:

- **Short-term health plans**: Lower premiums, but limited coverage and they don't cover pre-existing conditions. Not a replacement for comprehensive coverage.
- **Health sharing ministries**: Religious-based cost-sharing groups. Not technically insurance, with no state guarantee of payment.
- **Association health plans**: If you're self-employed or own a small business, some trade associations offer group-rate plans.

These alternatives have real limitations. Before signing up for anything that sounds too good to be true, talk to a licensed agent who can explain exactly what you're getting — and what you're not.
