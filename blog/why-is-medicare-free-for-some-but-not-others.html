Why Is Medicare Free for Some But Not Others?

Published June 24, 2026

You've probably heard someone say, "Medicare is free once you turn 65." And you've probably also heard someone else complain about their Medicare premiums. So which is it? Is Medicare free or not?

The answer: it depends on which part of Medicare you're talking about — and whether you (or your spouse) paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years.

Let's break it down so you know exactly what to expect when you turn 65.

Medicare Part A: Usually Free (But Not Always)

Part A covers hospital stays, skilled nursing care, hospice, and some home health services. For most people, Part A is premium-free.

You get Part A for free if:

  • You (or your spouse) worked and paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters)
  • You're currently receiving Social Security or Railroad Retirement benefits
  • You worked long enough in a government job where you paid Medicare taxes

You'll pay a premium for Part A if:

  • You didn't work the required 10 years, and your spouse didn't either
  • You're under 65 and qualify for Medicare due to disability (but haven't received benefits for 24 months yet)

What you'd pay if Part A isn't free:

  • Less than 30 work quarters: $505/month (2024 rate)
  • 30-39 work quarters: $278/month (2024 rate)

Most people qualify for premium-free Part A. But even when Part A is free, you still pay deductibles and coinsurance when you use it. In 2024, the Part A deductible is $1,632 per hospital stay.

Medicare Part B: Never Free

Part B covers doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services, and medical equipment. Unlike Part A, everyone pays a monthly premium for Part B — even if you worked 40 years and paid Medicare taxes.

Standard Part B premium in 2024: $174.70/month

But here's the catch: if you earn more than $103,000 as an individual (or $206,000 as a couple), you'll pay more. This is called an Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount (IRMAA).

High earners can pay anywhere from $244.60 to $594.00 per month for Part B, depending on income.

Part B also has a $240 annual deductible (2024), and you typically pay 20% coinsurance for most services.

Medicare Part D: Also Not Free

Part D covers prescription drugs. If you want drug coverage through Medicare, you'll pay a monthly premium for a standalone Part D plan.

Average Part D premium in 2024: $55/month (varies by plan)

Just like Part B, high earners pay IRMAA surcharges on Part D. If your income is above the threshold, you'll pay an extra $13.30 to $81.00 per month on top of your plan premium.

So, Is Medicare Really Free?

Here's the truth:

  • Part A: Free for most people (if you worked 10+ years)
  • Part B: $174.70/month minimum (more for high earners)
  • Part D: $55/month average (varies by plan)

Even when Part A is free, you still pay:

  • Deductibles (Part A: $1,632, Part B: $240)
  • Coinsurance (typically 20% for Part B services)
  • Copays (for doctor visits, prescriptions, etc.)

Bottom line: Medicare is not completely free for most people. You'll pay at least $175/month for Part B, plus out-of-pocket costs when you use your benefits.

Medicare Advantage and Medicare Supplement: What About Those?

Many people choose Medicare Advantage (Part C) or Medicare Supplement (Medigap) plans to reduce out-of-pocket costs.

Medicare Advantage:

  • Replaces Original Medicare (Parts A & B)
  • Often includes Part D drug coverage
  • Some plans have $0 premiums (but you still pay the Part B premium to Medicare)
  • Typically lower out-of-pocket costs than Original Medicare
  • Network restrictions apply

Medicare Supplement (Medigap):

  • Works with Original Medicare
  • Covers gaps like deductibles and coinsurance
  • Monthly premium (varies by plan and ZIP code)
  • Freedom to see any doctor who accepts Medicare
  • Does NOT include drug coverage (you'd add a Part D plan)

Both options require the Part B premium ($174.70/month minimum). Medicare Advantage may have an additional plan premium (or $0). Medigap always has a premium, typically $100–$300/month in Florida.

Why Do Some People Think Medicare Is Free?

Two main reasons:

1. They had employer insurance before Medicare
If you had employer health insurance your whole career, you might not have paid attention to premiums — your employer covered most or all of it. When you switch to Medicare, suddenly you're paying $175+/month directly, and it feels expensive.

2. They only looked at Part A
Part A is free for most people, so some assume the whole program is free. Then they're surprised when the Part B bill arrives.

What If I Can't Afford Medicare?

If you're struggling to pay Medicare premiums and out-of-pocket costs, you may qualify for help.

Medicare Savings Programs (MSP):

  • Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB): Covers Part A and Part B premiums, plus deductibles and coinsurance
  • Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB): Covers Part B premium only
  • Qualifying Individual (QI): Covers Part B premium only

Income limits vary by program and household size. In Florida, QMB covers individuals earning up to about $1,275/month (2024).

Extra Help (Part D Low-Income Subsidy):

  • Reduces or eliminates Part D premiums and drug copays
  • Income limits are higher than MSP programs

How to apply:

  • Medicare Savings Programs: Apply through your state Medicaid office
  • Extra Help: Apply at ssa.gov or call Social Security

FAQs: Is Medicare Free?

Q: Is Medicare Part A free?
A: Yes, for most people who worked 10+ years and paid Medicare taxes. If you didn't work long enough, you'll pay a premium.
Q: Is Medicare Part B free?
A: No. Everyone pays at least $174.70/month (2024 rate). High earners pay more.
Q: Do I have to pay for Medicare if I'm still working at 65?
A: It depends. If your employer has 20+ employees and offers health insurance, you can delay Part B (and avoid the premium) until you retire. But you should still enroll in Part A if it's free.
Q: Can I get financial help to pay for Medicare?
A: Yes. Medicare Savings Programs and Extra Help are available for low-income beneficiaries. Check if you qualify.
Q: Is Medicare Advantage free?
A: Some plans have $0 plan premiums, but you still pay the Part B premium ($174.70/month) to Medicare. You'll also have copays when you use care.
