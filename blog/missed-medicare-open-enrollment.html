What Happens If You Miss Medicare Open Enrollment?

The Health Experts Insurance · Medicare Basics

It happens more often than you'd think. Life gets busy — the holidays, the grandkids, the traffic on the Palmetto — and suddenly it's December 8 and you realize you never reviewed your Medicare coverage. Take a breath. Missing the Annual Enrollment Period isn't the end of the world, but it does change what your options look like. Here's what actually happens next.

First, what you missed: the Annual Enrollment Period

The Annual Enrollment Period (AEP) runs every year from October 15 through December 7. During AEP, anyone with Medicare can join, switch, or drop a Medicare Advantage plan or Part D prescription drug plan, with changes taking effect January 1.

If you didn't make a change during AEP, in most cases your current plan simply renews for the new year. That can be perfectly fine — or it can be a problem if your plan changed its costs, network, or drug formulary and you didn't notice.

You may still have options after December 7

1. Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (January 1 – March 31). If you're already in a Medicare Advantage plan, you get one chance during this window to switch to a different Medicare Advantage plan or return to Original Medicare (and add a Part D plan). Note: this window is only for people already enrolled in Medicare Advantage — you can't use it to jump from Original Medicare into a Medicare Advantage plan.

2. Special Enrollment Periods (SEPs). Certain life events open a window to make changes any time of year, including:

  • Moving outside your plan's service area (or moving into a new one)
  • Losing employer or union coverage
  • Qualifying for Medicaid, a Medicare Savings Program, or Extra Help with prescription costs
  • Your plan leaving your county or being sanctioned by Medicare
  • Living in, entering, or leaving a nursing facility

Many of our South Florida clients qualify for an SEP without realizing it — especially those who qualify for both Medicare and Medicaid.

Watch out for late enrollment penalties

Missing AEP is different from missing your initial enrollment in Medicare itself. If you delayed Part B without creditable employer coverage, or went 63 days or more without creditable prescription drug coverage, Medicare can add a late enrollment penalty to your premium — and the Part D penalty typically lasts for as long as you have drug coverage. We break down how these penalties work on our Medicare penalties FAQ page.

What to do right now

  • Review your plan's Annual Notice of Change (ANOC) so you know what changed for this year.
  • Confirm your doctors and prescriptions are still covered.
  • Check whether you qualify for an SEP or for a Medicare Savings Program.
  • Mark October 15 on your calendar so next AEP doesn't sneak up on you.

Frequently asked questions

What happens if I miss the Medicare Annual Enrollment Period?

Your current coverage generally continues into the new year. To make changes, you may need to wait for the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period (January 1 – March 31) or qualify for a Special Enrollment Period.

Can I still change my Medicare Advantage plan after December 7?

If you're already enrolled in a Medicare Advantage plan, yes — one change is allowed between January 1 and March 31 during the Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment Period.

What is a Special Enrollment Period?

An enrollment window triggered by certain life events — like moving, losing employer coverage, or qualifying for Medicaid or Extra Help — that lets you make changes outside normal enrollment periods.

Will I pay a penalty if I missed Medicare enrollment?

Possibly, if you delayed Part B or went without creditable drug coverage. Penalties are added to your monthly premium and, for Part D, usually last as long as you have drug coverage.
