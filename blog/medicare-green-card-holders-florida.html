Can Green Card Holders Get Medicare in Florida? What You Need to Know

South Florida is home to one of the most diverse immigrant communities in the United States. Miami-Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties have large populations of Cuban, Haitian, Venezuelan, Colombian, Nicaraguan, and Brazilian residents — many of whom are lawful permanent residents (green card holders) who have built their lives here and are now approaching Medicare age.

One of the most common questions we hear from clients in our community is: "I have a green card — am I eligible for Medicare?"

The short answer is: it depends. Let's break it down clearly.

The Basic Medicare Eligibility Rules for Green Card Holders

Medicare is primarily a federal program for people 65 and older. US citizens automatically meet the citizenship/residency requirement. For lawful permanent residents (LPRs), there are two key requirements:

1. The 5-Year Rule

To qualify for Medicare, a green card holder must have been a lawful permanent resident for at least 5 continuous years. This is sometimes called the "5-year bar."

  • If you got your green card 5+ years ago and are now 65 or older — you likely meet this requirement.
  • If you got your green card less than 5 years ago — you may need to wait before you can enroll.

There are limited exceptions to this rule (such as for certain refugees and asylees), but most green card holders must satisfy this waiting period.

2. Work Credits (For Premium-Free Part A)

Medicare Part A — hospital insurance — is free for most people because they (or their spouse) paid into Medicare through payroll taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters) of work in the United States.

If you or your spouse worked in the US for at least 10 years and paid Medicare taxes, you may qualify for premium-free Part A, just like a US citizen would.

If you haven't worked long enough to earn those 40 quarters, you can still get Medicare — but you'll pay a monthly premium for Part A, which can be over $500/month in 2026.

Four Scenarios Common Among Florida Immigrants

Scenario 1: You've Had a Green Card for 5+ Years and Worked 10+ Years in the US

You're in great shape. You likely qualify for full Medicare benefits, including premium-free Part A. Enroll during your Initial Enrollment Period — the 7-month window around your 65th birthday.

Scenario 2: You've Had a Green Card for 5+ Years but Worked Fewer Than 10 Years

You still qualify for Medicare, but may pay premiums for Part A. Part B has a standard monthly premium regardless of work history. This scenario is common for people who immigrated later in life or whose work was not covered by Social Security (e.g., self-employment where taxes weren't filed).

Scenario 3: You Got Your Green Card Recently (Less Than 5 Years Ago)

You are not yet eligible for Medicare. You'll need to wait until you've been a lawful permanent resident for 5 continuous years. In the meantime, explore ACA Marketplace plans through HealthCare.gov — you may qualify for subsidies based on income.

Scenario 4: You Have Citizenship from a Country with a US Social Security Totalization Agreement

Some countries have bilateral agreements with the US that allow foreign work credits to count toward US Social Security and Medicare eligibility. The US has agreements with about 30 countries — primarily in Europe plus Australia, Brazil, Canada, Chile, Japan, South Korea, and Uruguay. Note: Mexico, Cuba, and Colombia do NOT currently have Totalization Agreements with the US. If you worked in one of the covered countries before immigrating, your foreign credits may count toward Medicare eligibility. This is a complex area — speak with a licensed agent or Social Security benefits counselor to see if this applies to you.

Medicare Parts Explained for New Enrollees

Once you confirm you're eligible, here's what you're enrolling in:

  • Part A — Hospital insurance. Covers inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, hospice. Free if you have 40 work quarters; otherwise you pay a premium.
  • Part B — Medical insurance. Covers doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services. Standard premium applies ($202.90/month in 2026).
  • Part C (Medicare Advantage) — A private plan alternative that bundles A + B (and usually D) with potential extra benefits.
  • Part D — Prescription drug coverage. Comes with Medicare Advantage or as a standalone plan.
  • Medigap — Supplemental insurance to cover Part A and B cost-sharing gaps.

Enrolling in Medicare: Timing Matters in Florida

Missing your enrollment window can result in late enrollment penalties that last for life. Here are the key windows:

  • Initial Enrollment Period (IEP): 7-month window — 3 months before, your birthday month, and 3 months after turning 65.
  • General Enrollment Period (GEP): January 1 – March 31 each year, for those who missed their IEP. Coverage begins the first day of the month after you enroll (per the BENES Act, effective 2023).
  • Special Enrollment Periods (SEPs): If you're still working and covered by an employer plan, or if you experience a life event like losing other coverage.

Late enrollment in Part B carries a 10% premium penalty for every full 12-month period you delayed. That adds up fast.

Common Questions from South Florida Immigrants

"Do I need to speak English to enroll?" No. Our agents at The Health Experts Insurance serve clients in English and Spanish, and we help navigate the Medicare enrollment process from start to finish.

"Can I keep my Cuban or Venezuelan doctor?" It depends on the plan. Medicare Advantage networks vary. A licensed agent can help you find plans that include your preferred providers.

"What about my spouse who is younger than 65?" If your spouse is under 65 and doesn't yet qualify for Medicare, they'll need separate coverage — through an employer plan, the ACA Marketplace, or Medicaid if eligible.

"I pay into Medicare at work — does that count toward my eligibility?" Yes! Every quarter you work and pay Medicare taxes counts toward your 40-quarter requirement, regardless of your immigration status (as long as you're authorized to work).
