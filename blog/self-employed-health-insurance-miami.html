Self-Employed in Miami? Here's How to Get Health Insurance

Published June 22, 2026 | Health Experts

Being self-employed in Miami comes with a lot of freedom — setting your own hours, choosing your clients, building something that's yours. But there's one thing that can trip up even the most prepared freelancer or business owner: health insurance.

When you work for an employer, someone else handles your health coverage. When you work for yourself, that responsibility lands entirely on you. And with health insurance costs in Florida ranging from $0 to $800+ per month depending on your income and plan, the stakes are real.

The good news? Self-employed people in Miami actually have more options than most people realize. You just need to know where to look.

Your Options as a Self-Employed Person in Miami

There's no single "right" answer for self-employed health insurance. The best plan depends on your income, your health needs, your family situation, and whether you qualify for financial assistance. Here's what's available to you:

1. ACA Marketplace Plans (Healthcare.gov)

The Affordable Care Act Marketplace is often the first place self-employed people should look. If your income is between 100% and 400% of the federal poverty level (and in some cases higher), you may qualify for Premium Tax Credits that significantly reduce your monthly cost.

For a single self-employed person in Miami earning $40,000/year in 2026, this could mean paying as little as $0–$150/month for a solid silver plan. These plans cover pre-existing conditions, include prescription drug coverage, and include all 10 essential health benefits required by law.

The challenge? Open Enrollment only runs from November 1 to January 15. Outside of that window, you can only enroll if you have a qualifying life event — like losing other coverage or getting married. However, if your income fluctuates (as it often does for freelancers), you may qualify for a Special Enrollment Period based on income changes.

2. Private Health Insurance

If your income is too high for ACA subsidies — or if you simply want more flexibility in when you can enroll — private health insurance may be the better fit. Private plans are purchased directly from an insurance carrier and are available year-round.

The trade-off: private plans may not cover pre-existing conditions the same way ACA plans do, and you won't receive subsidies. But for healthy adults with higher incomes, they can be significantly more affordable and flexible than subsidized ACA plans.

3. Short-Term Health Plans

Need coverage fast — like, tomorrow — while you figure out a longer-term solution? Short-term health plans can be activated in as little as 24 hours. They're cheaper, but they come with limitations: they don't cover pre-existing conditions, and they're not ACA-compliant.

These work best as bridge coverage — a gap-filler while you wait for Open Enrollment or sort out your permanent plan.

4. Small Group Health Plans

If you have at least one employee (even a spouse on payroll), you may qualify for a small group health plan. These plans are typically more comprehensive than individual plans, and your premium contributions as an employer may be tax-deductible.

The Tax Advantage No One Talks About

Here's something worth knowing: self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of their health insurance premiums from their federal income taxes. That means if you're paying $300/month for health insurance, you may be able to deduct $3,600/year — reducing your taxable income significantly.

This makes private health insurance or ACA plans even more attractive for freelancers and business owners. Always consult your tax advisor for specifics, but this deduction applies to premiums paid for yourself, your spouse, and your dependents.

How to Choose the Right Plan for Your Situation

With so many options, how do you know which one is right for you? The answer depends on a few key factors:

  • Your estimated annual income — This determines whether you qualify for ACA subsidies and at what level
  • Your health situation — Do you have prescriptions? Ongoing care? Specialists you need to keep?
  • Your family — Are you covering just yourself, or a spouse and kids?
  • Your cash flow — Can you handle a higher deductible for a lower monthly premium, or do you need predictable costs?

This is exactly where a licensed independent broker becomes invaluable. A broker can run the numbers for you, compare every available plan, and tell you honestly whether the ACA Marketplace or private insurance is the better fit for your income and needs — completely free.

Open Enrollment Timing for Self-Employed

If you're planning to enroll through the ACA Marketplace, mark your calendar:

  • Open Enrollment: November 1 – January 15
  • Coverage starts: January 1 (if enrolled by December 15) or February 1
  • Special Enrollment: Year-round if you have a qualifying event

Private health insurance is available year-round — no enrollment windows to worry about.
