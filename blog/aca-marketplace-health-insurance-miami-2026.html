ACA Health Insurance in Miami: Your 2026 Guide to Plans, Costs & Subsidies

If you don’t have health insurance through a job or Medicare, the ACA Marketplace — also called the Health Insurance Marketplace or Exchange — is where most Miami-Dade and Broward residents can find affordable coverage. And in 2026, subsidies are significantly better than they were a few years ago, meaning many people qualify for lower premiums than they expect.

Here’s what you actually need to know.

What Is the ACA Marketplace and Who Can Use It?

The ACA Marketplace is a government-run system where private insurance companies offer plans that meet federal coverage standards. In Florida, the federal marketplace at healthcare.gov is where you shop.

You can use it if you:

  • Are a U.S. citizen or lawfully present resident
  • Live in Florida
  • Are not incarcerated
  • Are not eligible for Medicare (if you’re 65+, Medicare is usually your route)
  • Don’t have access to affordable job-based coverage that meets minimum standards

There’s no income floor to enroll — but your income affects whether you qualify for subsidies that reduce your monthly premium.

How Do Subsidies Work in 2026?

The federal government offers two types of financial help:

1. Premium Tax Credits (PTCs) — reduce your monthly premium. In 2026, these credits are available to households earning between 100% and 400% of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL). That means a single person earning up to roughly $62,600/year, or a family of four earning up to $128,400/year, may qualify.

2. Cost-Sharing Reductions (CSRs) — lower your deductibles and out-of-pocket costs. These only apply if you choose a Silver plan and your income is below 250% FPL (roughly $39,000 for a single person).

One important change since 2021: enhanced subsidies have made coverage more accessible, and many people who previously assumed they earned too much are now qualifying for meaningful premium reductions.

What Plans Are Available in Miami-Dade and Broward?

ACA plans in South Florida are organized into four metal tiers:

  • Bronze — lowest monthly premium, higher out-of-pocket costs
  • Silver — mid-range premium, eligible for cost-sharing reductions
  • Gold — higher premium, lower out-of-pocket costs when you use care
  • Platinum — highest premium, lowest cost-sharing

Most carriers in the South Florida marketplace offer HMO plans — meaning you’ll select a primary care doctor and need referrals for specialists. Network size varies significantly by carrier, which matters if you have established doctors.

When Can You Enroll?

The main enrollment window — Open Enrollment — runs from November 1 through January 15 each year. Coverage for plans selected by December 15 starts January 1.

Outside that window, you can still enroll if you have a Special Enrollment Period (SEP) triggered by a qualifying life event:

  • Losing job-based coverage
  • Moving to a new area
  • Getting married or divorced
  • Having a baby or adopting
  • Losing Medicaid or CHIP coverage
  • Turning 26 and aging off a parent’s plan

You generally have 60 days from the qualifying event to enroll. Missing that window means waiting until November.

What Does Coverage Actually Cost in Miami?

Premiums vary by age, plan tier, tobacco use, and your subsidy amount. As a rough benchmark in 2026:

A 45-year-old in Miami-Dade enrolling in a Silver plan might pay anywhere from $0 to $350/month depending on their income and subsidy. A 60-year-old could pay $0 to $700+/month for the same Silver plan — age increases the base premium but subsidies can offset a large portion of it.

The only way to know your actual cost is to run your income through the calculator at healthcare.gov or have a licensed broker check it for you.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Not enrolling because you assume it’s too expensive. With enhanced subsidies, many people qualify for plans under $100/month or even $0/month.
  2. Picking the cheapest plan without checking the network. Low-premium Bronze plans often have narrow networks — make sure your doctors and preferred hospitals are included.
  3. Forgetting to report income changes mid-year. If your income changes significantly, update your application. Underpaying subsidies now means a repayment at tax time.
  4. Missing the SEP window. You have 60 days from a qualifying event. Don’t wait.

How a Local Broker Can Help

Shopping the ACA Marketplace on your own is possible, but a licensed local broker can:

  • Run your subsidy estimate instantly
  • Compare every available plan in your zip code — not just the ones that appear first
  • Make sure your current doctors are in-network before you enroll
  • Help you with SEP paperwork if you had a qualifying event

And the cost to you is the same whether you use a broker or not — brokers are paid by the insurance company, not by you.
