Medicare Enrollment Periods Explained: When Can You Actually Make Changes?

One of the most common calls we get at our Doral office starts the same way: “I want to change my Medicare plan — can I do that now?”

The honest answer is: it depends on which enrollment window you’re in. Medicare doesn’t let you switch plans whenever you want, and missing a window can mean waiting months — or paying a permanent penalty. Here’s every enrollment period, in plain English.

Your Initial Enrollment Period (IEP): the 7-month window around your 65th birthday

Your first chance to enroll in Medicare is a 7-month window: the 3 months before the month you turn 65, your birthday month, and the 3 months after.

Two things Miami seniors get wrong all the time:

  1. Enrollment isn’t always automatic. If you’re already collecting Social Security, you’ll be enrolled in Parts A and B automatically. If you’re not — and more people are delaying Social Security every year — you have to sign up yourself through Social Security.
  2. Timing inside the window matters. Sign up during the 3 months before your birthday month and coverage starts the first day of your birthday month. Sign up later in the window and coverage starts the month after you enroll.

If you (or your spouse) are still working with employer coverage from a company with 20+ employees, you can usually delay Part B without penalty — but confirm this before your birthday, not after. Our new-to-Medicare guide walks through it, or schedule a free consultation and we’ll map your exact dates.

Annual Enrollment Period (AEP): October 15 – December 7

This is the big one. Every year during AEP, anyone on Medicare can:

  • Switch from Original Medicare to Medicare Advantage (or back)
  • Switch from one Medicare Advantage plan to another
  • Join, drop, or change a Part D drug plan

Changes take effect January 1. In late September, your plan mails you an Annual Notice of Change (ANOC) describing what your plan will cost and cover next year — read it, because plans change premiums, drug formularies, and doctor networks every single year. We review ANOC letters for clients at no charge. Details on this year’s window are on our Annual Enrollment page.

Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment (MA OEP): January 1 – March 31

If you’re already in a Medicare Advantage plan on January 1 and regret your choice, you get one switch during this window — to a different Medicare Advantage plan, or back to Original Medicare (with the option to add a Part D plan). One change only, and you can’t use it to move from Original Medicare into Medicare Advantage.

General Enrollment Period (GEP): January 1 – March 31

Missed your Initial Enrollment Period entirely and don’t qualify for a Special Enrollment Period? You can sign up for Part B from January 1 to March 31, with coverage starting the month after you enroll. The catch: you may owe a late enrollment penalty of 10% per year you delayed — for life. This is the window we try to keep clients out of.

Special Enrollment Periods (SEPs): life events open a window

Certain events let you enroll or switch outside the normal calendar:

  • Losing employer coverage: you get 8 months to sign up for Part B penalty-free, but only about 2 months to pick a Medicare Advantage or Part D plan. (Important: COBRA does not count as employer coverage for this purpose.)
  • Moving out of your plan’s service area — even from Miami-Dade to another Florida county can trigger this.
  • Your plan leaves the market, like AvMed’s Florida exit did.
  • Medicare + Medicaid or Extra Help: dual-eligible and low-income-subsidy beneficiaries have monthly opportunities to change to certain plans — more on dual eligibility here.
  • 5-star plans: if a 5-star rated plan is available in your county, you can switch to it once per year outside AEP.

Medigap has its own clock — and it only runs once

Your Medicare Supplement Open Enrollment is the 6 months starting the month you’re 65+ and enrolled in Part B. During those 6 months, no company can deny you or charge you more for health conditions. In Florida, once that window closes, most Medigap applications go through medical underwriting — you can be declined. If a Medicare Supplement plan is on your radar, this deadline matters more than any other on this page.

The bottom line

Window Dates Who it’s for
IEP 7 months around your 65th birthday New to Medicare
AEP Oct 15 – Dec 7 Everyone on Medicare
MA OEP Jan 1 – Mar 31 Already on Medicare Advantage (1 switch)
GEP Jan 1 – Mar 31 Missed IEP, no SEP
SEP Anytime a qualifying event happens Job loss, moves, plan exits, dual/LIS
Medigap OE 6 months from Part B start One-time, no health questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I change my Medicare plan anytime I want? No. Outside of the Annual Enrollment Period (October 15 – December 7), you can only make changes if you qualify for a Special Enrollment Period or are within another defined window, like Medicare Advantage Open Enrollment.

What happens if I miss my Initial Enrollment Period? You may have to wait for the General Enrollment Period (January 1 – March 31) and could owe a lifetime late enrollment penalty of 10% of the Part B premium for every 12 months you delayed without other creditable coverage.

Does COBRA count as employer coverage for delaying Medicare? No. COBRA is not considered active employer coverage. Your 8-month Part B Special Enrollment Period starts when you stop working — not when your COBRA ends.

Can I switch Medicare Advantage plans in January? Yes — if you’re already enrolled in a Medicare Advantage plan, the MA Open Enrollment Period (January 1 – March 31) allows one switch to another MA plan or back to Original Medicare.

When can I buy a Medicare Supplement plan without health questions? During your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period, which begins the month you are 65 or older and enrolled in Part B. After it ends, Florida insurers can require medical underwriting.
