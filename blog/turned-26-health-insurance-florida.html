Just Turned 26 in Florida? Here's What to Do About Health Insurance

Published June 24, 2026 | Health Experts

Turning 26 is a milestone — and in the world of health insurance, it's one of the most important birthdays you'll ever have. On your 26th birthday, you officially age off your parents' health insurance plan. And that means you have a limited window to get covered on your own.

If you're in Florida and just turned 26 (or you're approaching it), here's exactly what you need to know — and what you need to do.

What Happens When You Turn 26?

Under the Affordable Care Act, parents can keep their children on their health insurance plan up to age 26. Once you hit that birthday, most plans terminate your coverage at the end of your birth month (though some extend to the end of the year — check your parents' plan details).

Losing coverage this way triggers a Special Enrollment Period (SEP). This is important: it means you don't have to wait for Open Enrollment to get coverage. You have 60 days from the date you lose your parents' coverage to enroll in a new plan.

Don't let those 60 days slip by. Going without health insurance — even for a month — leaves you financially exposed. One emergency room visit in Miami can cost $5,000 or more without coverage.

Your Options at 26 in Florida

Option 1: ACA Marketplace Plan

If you don't have employer coverage, the ACA Marketplace is usually the best starting point. Depending on your income, you may qualify for significant subsidies that reduce your premium dramatically — sometimes to $0 or close to it.

For 2026, a single person in Miami earning up to $60,000/year may qualify for Premium Tax Credits. If you're just starting out in your career, your income might qualify you for a substantial discount.

Option 2: Employer Health Insurance

If you work for an employer that offers health insurance, this is often the most cost-effective choice. Your employer may cover 50–80% of the premium, making it much cheaper than buying on your own. Ask your HR department about enrollment timelines — losing parental coverage qualifies as a life event that lets you enroll outside the standard open enrollment window.

Option 3: Private Health Insurance

For young, healthy adults who don't qualify for large ACA subsidies — or who want year-round enrollment flexibility — private health insurance can be a good option. Plans are available directly from carriers and can be activated quickly.

Option 4: Medicaid (if eligible)

If your income is at or below 138% of the federal poverty level (~$20,783 for a single person in 2026), you may qualify for Florida Medicaid. Note: Florida has not expanded Medicaid under the ACA, so eligibility requirements here are stricter than in many other states.

What Does Health Insurance Cost at 26 in Florida?

For a healthy 26-year-old in Miami, here's what you might realistically pay in 2026:

  • ACA Bronze plan with subsidy: $0–$75/month (if income qualifies)
  • ACA Silver plan with subsidy: $50–$200/month
  • Private health plan (no subsidy): $150–$350/month
  • Employer plan (your share): $50–$200/month, depending on employer

The key variable is your income. If you just started working and your salary is moderate, you may qualify for more financial help than you expect.

What to Look for in Your First Health Plan

At 26, most people are healthy — but that doesn't mean you should grab the cheapest plan without thinking. Here's what actually matters:

  • Your doctors and hospitals — Does the plan cover your existing doctors or a hospital you'd want to use in an emergency?
  • Prescriptions — If you take any medications regularly, check the formulary before enrolling
  • The deductible — A $0/month premium with a $7,000 deductible may not serve you well if you get sick
  • Mental health coverage — All ACA plans are required to include mental health benefits

How to Enroll

Once you know your options, enrolling is straightforward. Here's the process:

  1. Confirm when your parents' coverage ends
  2. Gather your income information (pay stubs, tax return, or estimated annual income)
  3. Compare plans — either on Healthcare.gov or through a licensed broker
  4. Enroll before your 60-day Special Enrollment Period expires

If you miss the 60-day window after losing parental coverage, your next opportunity to enroll in an ACA plan is Open Enrollment (November 1 – January 15) — and you could be uninsured for months. Don't wait.
