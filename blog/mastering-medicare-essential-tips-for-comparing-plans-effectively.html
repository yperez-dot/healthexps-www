Mastering Medicare: Essential Tips for Comparing Plans Effectively

Published May 4, 2025

With more than 65 million Americans now enrolled in Medicare, understanding how to compare your options has never been more important. Whether you are turning 65 for the first time or re-evaluating your coverage during the Annual Enrollment Period, the sheer number of plan choices can feel overwhelming. The good news: a structured approach makes the process manageable. This guide walks you through the essential tips every Medicare beneficiary should know before making a decision.

Start With the Foundation: Original Medicare vs. Medicare Advantage

Before comparing individual plans, you need to understand the two main paths available to you.

Original Medicare (Parts A and B) is the federal program administered directly by the government. Part A covers hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, and some home health services. Part B covers outpatient care, doctor visits, and preventive services. Original Medicare gives you broad access to any provider that accepts Medicare nationwide — but it does not cap your out-of-pocket spending and does not include prescription drug coverage by default.

Medicare Advantage (Part C) is an alternative offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare. These plans bundle Parts A and B into a single plan, often include Part D drug coverage, and may offer extras like dental, vision, and hearing benefits. In exchange, you typically work within a defined network of providers and face different cost structures.

Neither option is universally better. Your health needs, preferred doctors, budget, and how often you travel all factor into which path makes more sense for you.

Check Whether Your Doctors Are In-Network

This is one of the most overlooked steps — and one of the most consequential. If you enroll in a Medicare Advantage plan, your access to specific doctors depends heavily on the plan's network.

  • HMO plans generally require you to use in-network providers and get referrals to see specialists.
  • PPO plans offer more flexibility, allowing you to see out-of-network providers, but usually at a higher cost.
  • PFFS and SNP plans have their own network and eligibility rules.

Before enrolling, verify that your primary care physician, specialists, and any preferred hospitals participate in the plan's network. Provider directories can change year to year, so confirm directly with the provider's office as well — not just the plan's website.

Look Beyond the Premium: Total Cost Is What Matters

A $0 monthly premium is eye-catching, but it does not tell the full story. When comparing plans, you need to add up all the moving parts:

  • Monthly premium — what you pay every month regardless of whether you use services
  • Annual deductible — what you pay out-of-pocket before the plan kicks in
  • Copays and coinsurance — your share of each doctor visit, specialist, or procedure
  • Maximum out-of-pocket limit — the annual cap on your cost-sharing (Medicare Advantage plans must have one; Original Medicare does not)

A plan with a low premium but high copays and a high out-of-pocket maximum could cost significantly more than a plan with a modest premium and lower cost-sharing if you need frequent medical care. Run the numbers for your expected healthcare usage, not just the best-case scenario.

Compare Drug Formularies Carefully (Part D)

If you take prescription medications — and most Medicare beneficiaries do — drug coverage deserves its own focused review. Each Part D plan (whether standalone or bundled into a Medicare Advantage plan) uses a formulary, which is its list of covered drugs.

Key things to verify for every plan you are considering:

  • Are all your current medications on the formulary?
  • What tier are your drugs placed in? Higher tiers typically mean higher cost-sharing.
  • Does the plan require prior authorization or step therapy for any of your medications?
  • Which pharmacies are in the plan's preferred network? Using a preferred pharmacy can lower your costs substantially.

Formularies can change annually, so even if you were satisfied with your drug coverage last year, always re-check before the new plan year begins.

Pay Attention to CMS Star Ratings

The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) evaluates Medicare Advantage and Part D plans each year on a scale of 1 to 5 stars. These ratings measure plan quality across categories including customer service, member experience, managing chronic conditions, and drug plan safety.

A plan rated 4 or 5 stars generally indicates strong performance and member satisfaction. Plans rated below 3 stars may have documented issues worth investigating. Star ratings are publicly available and should be one of the factors you weigh — not the only one, but a meaningful quality signal.

Understand Out-of-Network Costs for Medicare Advantage Plans

If you travel frequently, spend time in multiple states, or want the freedom to see any provider, out-of-network costs are a critical consideration. With many Medicare Advantage HMO plans, non-emergency out-of-network care may not be covered at all except in emergencies. PPO plans cover out-of-network care, but at a higher cost-sharing level that can add up quickly.

By contrast, Original Medicare allows you to see any provider that accepts Medicare anywhere in the country — giving you maximum flexibility if you split time between locations.

Consider Medigap to Fill the Gaps in Original Medicare

If you choose Original Medicare, you will likely face unpredictable out-of-pocket costs since there is no annual cap. A Medicare Supplement plan (Medigap) is private coverage designed to pay for costs that Original Medicare does not cover — such as hospital deductibles, coinsurance, and in some cases foreign travel emergencies.

Medigap plans are standardized by the federal government (labeled Plan A through Plan N), meaning the benefits for a given letter are the same across insurance companies. What differs is the monthly premium. If you want predictable healthcare costs and broad provider access, pairing Original Medicare with a Medigap plan and a standalone Part D plan is a common approach.

Use the Medicare Plan Finder Tool

The official Medicare Plan Finder at Medicare.gov is the most reliable starting point for comparing plans available in your area. You can enter your zip code and medications to see side-by-side estimates of total annual costs, including premiums, deductibles, and expected drug costs based on your prescriptions. The tool also displays star ratings for each plan.

Use the Plan Finder to narrow your options, then verify the details — especially provider networks and formulary specifics — directly with the plans you are seriously considering.

Know Your Enrollment Windows

Timing matters. The Annual Enrollment Period (AEP) runs from October 15 through December 7 each year. During this window, any Medicare beneficiary can switch between Original Medicare and Medicare Advantage, change Medicare Advantage plans, or join, switch, or drop a Part D drug plan. Changes made during AEP take effect January 1 of the following year.

If you are new to Medicare, your Initial Enrollment Period spans the seven months around your 65th birthday. Missing this window without qualifying for a Special Enrollment Period can result in late enrollment penalties, so mark these dates on your calendar.
