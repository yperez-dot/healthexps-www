The Hidden Treasure: How Medicare Savings Programs Slash Your Health Insurance Costs

Published May 11, 2025

If you have Medicare and a limited income, you could be sitting on a financial lifeline you've never used — and may not even know exists. Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs) are federal-and-state-funded programs that help pay for Medicare costs such as premiums, deductibles, and copayments. Yet millions of eligible Americans are not enrolled. The reason? No one told them about it.

This guide explains the four MSP levels, who qualifies, what benefits each one provides, and how to apply in Florida — at absolutely no cost to you.

What Are Medicare Savings Programs?

Medicare Savings Programs are a category of Medicaid benefits specifically designed to reduce the out-of-pocket costs that come with Original Medicare (Parts A and B). They are administered jointly by the federal government and each state. In Florida, the program is run by the Department of Children and Families (DCF).

There are four distinct MSP levels, each covering different costs and targeting slightly different income brackets. Importantly, the income limits for these programs are higher than those for standard Medicaid — meaning many people who do not qualify for Medicaid still qualify for an MSP.

The Four Levels of Medicare Savings Programs

1. Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB) — The Most Comprehensive

QMB is the most generous level. If you qualify, the program covers:

  • Medicare Part A premium (if applicable)
  • Medicare Part B premium (approximately $202.90/month in 2026)
  • Part A and Part B deductibles
  • Part A and Part B coinsurance and copayments

For 2026, the individual income limit for QMB is approximately 100% of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL) — roughly $1,325 per month for an individual and $1,783 per month for a couple. Resource limits also apply but exclude your home, car, and certain life insurance policies.

An important legal protection: providers who accept Medicare cannot bill you for cost-sharing if you are enrolled in QMB. This is federal law — a protection many beneficiaries don't know they have.

2. Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB) — Part B Premium Paid

SLMB covers your Medicare Part B monthly premium. That's roughly $202.90 per month, or $2,220 per year, going straight back into your pocket. Income limits for SLMB fall between approximately 100% and 120% of the FPL — roughly $1,325 to $1,590 per month for an individual in 2026.

3. Qualifying Individual (QI) — Part B Premium Paid

The QI program also covers your Part B premium, but it serves people with incomes between approximately 120% and 135% of the FPL — roughly $1,590 to $1,792 per month for an individual. One distinction: QI is funded through annual federal appropriations, so enrollment is on a first-come, first-served basis each year. It pays to apply early.

4. Qualified Disabled and Working Individuals (QDWI) — For Working Disabled Adults

QDWI is a lesser-known program for individuals under 65 who are disabled, working, and lost their premium-free Part A coverage when they returned to work. QDWI covers the Part A premium for these individuals. Income limits are higher — up to 200% of the FPL — but resource limits are stricter. If you or a family member fits this profile, it is worth exploring.

You May Qualify Without Knowing It

One of the biggest misconceptions is that MSPs are only for people in extreme poverty. That is not the case. The income thresholds — particularly for SLMB and QI — are set at levels that include many retired individuals who live on Social Security alone or on a modest pension. If your monthly income is under about $1,800 (individual) or $2,400 (couple), you may well qualify for at least one program level.

Each year, an estimated 1 in 3 eligible people do not enroll in an MSP simply because they were never informed. That is thousands of dollars in benefits left unclaimed.

Extra Help for Medicare Part D (Low Income Subsidy)

MSPs often work hand-in-hand with another program: Extra Help, also known as the Low Income Subsidy (LIS). Extra Help reduces what you pay for prescription drugs under Medicare Part D, including lower premiums, reduced deductibles, and minimal copays at the pharmacy counter.

If you qualify for QMB, SLMB, or QI, you are automatically eligible for Extra Help at the full subsidy level. This can save hundreds to thousands of dollars annually on medications. The Social Security Administration administers Extra Help, and you can apply through SSA.gov or by calling 1-800-772-1213.

How to Apply in Florida

In Florida, Medicare Savings Programs are administered through the Department of Children and Families (DCF). There are several ways to apply:

  • Online: Through the ACCESS Florida portal at myflorida.com/accessflorida
  • By phone: Call the DCF Customer Call Center at 1-866-762-2237
  • In person: Visit your local DCF office
  • Through a licensed broker: An agent can help you complete the process and ensure your application is accurate

Application is completely free. You will typically need to provide proof of identity, proof of Medicare enrollment (your red-white-and-blue Medicare card), proof of income (such as a Social Security award letter), and bank statements to verify resources.

Retroactive Coverage: Don't Wait

One often-overlooked benefit: MSP coverage can be applied retroactively in some cases. If you were eligible in prior months and did not know it, you may be able to recover premiums that were already deducted from your Social Security check. This is another reason to apply as soon as possible rather than waiting.

Income and Resource Limits Change Each Year

MSP income and resource limits are adjusted annually based on the Federal Poverty Level. Always verify the current year's thresholds when applying, as the exact dollar amounts shift each January. Your state SHIP (State Health Insurance Assistance Program) counselor — or a licensed Medicare agent — can give you the most current figures for your household situation.
