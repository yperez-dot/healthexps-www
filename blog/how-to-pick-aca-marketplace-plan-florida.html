How to Pick a Health Insurance Plan on the ACA Marketplace in Florida

Open enrollment season comes around every year, and every year millions of Floridians face the same challenge: staring at a screen full of health insurance plan options, trying to figure out which one actually makes sense for their family.

Bronze, Silver, Gold. $0 premium plans. Subsidies. Deductibles. Networks. It's a lot. And making the wrong choice can mean either overpaying every month or getting hit with a huge bill when you actually need care.

This guide walks you through how to pick an ACA Marketplace plan in Florida — clearly, without the jargon — so you can make a confident decision.

What Is the ACA Marketplace?

The Affordable Care Act (ACA) Marketplace — also called the Health Insurance Exchange — is the government-run platform where individuals and families who don't have coverage through an employer or government program (like Medicare or Medicaid) can shop for and enroll in health insurance.

In Florida, Marketplace plans are purchased through HealthCare.gov (Florida doesn't run its own state exchange). Plans are offered by private insurance companies but must meet minimum federal standards for coverage.

When Can You Enroll?

Open Enrollment Period

Open enrollment for ACA Marketplace plans in Florida typically runs from November 1 through January 15. If you enroll by December 15, your coverage starts January 1. Enrolling between December 16 and January 15 means coverage begins February 1.

Mark these dates. Missing open enrollment means waiting until the next year unless you have a qualifying life event.

Special Enrollment Periods (SEPs)

If you experience a qualifying life event, you may enroll outside of open enrollment. Common qualifying events in Florida include:

  • Losing other health coverage (job loss, losing Medicaid, aging off a parent's plan)
  • Getting married or divorced
  • Having a baby or adopting a child
  • Moving to a new ZIP code or county
  • Gaining lawful immigration status
  • Changes in household income that affect subsidy eligibility

SEPs typically give you a 60-day window to enroll after the qualifying event. Don't wait — the clock starts on the day of the event.

Understanding ACA Subsidies: Are You Eligible?

One of the most important things to know about the ACA Marketplace is that most Floridians who enroll receive financial help. Subsidies — formally called premium tax credits — are available based on your income.

Income Thresholds for 2026

ACA subsidies are available to individuals and families earning between 100% and 400% of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL). Enhanced subsidies under the Inflation Reduction Act extended eligibility further — even households above 400% FPL may qualify for reduced premiums if their plan costs exceed a certain percentage of their income.

General 2026 income guidelines (approximate):

Household Size 100% FPL 400% FPL
1 person ~$15,060 ~$60,240
2 people ~$20,440 ~$81,760
4 people ~$31,200 ~$124,800

Important: If you qualify for Medicaid, you're not eligible for Marketplace subsidies. If you have affordable employer coverage offered to you, your subsidy eligibility may be limited.

Cost-Sharing Reductions (CSRs)

If you earn between 100% and 250% of FPL and enroll in a Silver-tier plan, you may also qualify for cost-sharing reductions — extra help that lowers your deductibles, copays, and out-of-pocket maximums. This is a hidden gem many enrollees miss.

The Metal Tiers: Bronze, Silver, Gold, Platinum

ACA plans are categorized by "metal tiers" that describe how costs are split between you and the insurance company:

Bronze

  • Insurance pays: ~60% of costs
  • You pay: ~40% of costs
  • Premiums: Lowest
  • Deductibles: Highest ($6,000–$8,000 is common)
  • Best for: Healthy people who rarely use medical care and want the cheapest monthly premium with a safety net for emergencies

Silver

  • Insurance pays: ~70% of costs
  • You pay: ~30% of costs
  • Premiums: Moderate
  • Deductibles: Moderate
  • Best for: Most people — and the only tier that qualifies for cost-sharing reductions. If you're between 100–250% FPL, Silver is almost always your best value.

Gold

  • Insurance pays: ~80% of costs
  • You pay: ~20% of costs
  • Premiums: Higher
  • Deductibles: Lower
  • Best for: People who use healthcare regularly (prescriptions, specialist visits, ongoing conditions). Higher premiums are offset by lower out-of-pocket costs.

Platinum

  • Insurance pays: ~90% of costs
  • You pay: ~10% of costs
  • Premiums: Highest
  • Deductibles: Lowest (often $0)
  • Best for: People with high ongoing medical needs who want maximum cost predictability

Key Things to Check Before You Pick a Plan

1. Is Your Doctor In-Network?

Not all plans include the same providers. Check whether your primary care doctor, specialists, and preferred hospital are in-network before you enroll. An out-of-network visit can cost significantly more — or be uncovered entirely.

2. Are Your Prescriptions Covered?

Each plan has a formulary — a list of covered drugs. If you take regular medications, check that your prescriptions are on the plan's formulary and review the cost tier they fall under.

3. What Is the Out-of-Pocket Maximum?

This is the most you'll ever pay in a single year for covered services. Once you hit this limit, insurance covers 100%. For 2026, federal law caps out-of-pocket maximums at around $10,600 for individuals. Look for plans with lower caps if you anticipate significant healthcare use.

4. HMO vs. PPO vs. EPO

  • HMO (Health Maintenance Organization): Requires a primary care physician referral for specialists. Lower cost, more restricted network.
  • PPO (Preferred Provider Organization): More flexibility; see specialists without a referral. Higher premiums.
  • EPO (Exclusive Provider Organization): Like an HMO but without the referral requirement — but no out-of-network coverage (except emergencies).

Most Florida ACA plans are HMOs or EPOs. If you want flexibility to see any doctor, look specifically for PPO options.

Florida-Specific Considerations

Florida is one of the largest ACA Marketplace states in the country — and also one of the most competitive, which means more plan options but also more potential for confusion.

A few Florida-specific tips:

  • Miami-Dade and Broward have large multilingual populations. Some carriers offer dedicated Spanish-language member services and Spanish-speaking provider networks.
  • South Florida's healthcare market is dominated by a few large systems (Baptist Health, Cleveland Clinic Florida, HCA Florida). Confirm which system your chosen plan uses.
  • Uninsured rates in Florida remain high, partly because many residents don't know they qualify for subsidized coverage. If you've assumed you can't afford coverage, check again — the current subsidy structure may surprise you.
  • Medicaid gap: Florida has historically not expanded Medicaid under the ACA. If your income falls below 100% FPL, you may fall into a coverage gap where you're ineligible for both Medicaid and Marketplace subsidies. Speak with an agent to explore all options.

The Biggest Mistakes Florida Shoppers Make

  1. Picking the cheapest premium without looking at the deductible. A $0/month plan with a $7,500 deductible could cost you far more than a $150/month plan with a $1,500 deductible if you actually use care.

  2. Not checking the network. Finding out your doctor isn't covered after you've already enrolled is a costly mistake.

  3. Missing the subsidy. Many Floridians skip the subsidy application because they assume they don't qualify. Always run the numbers.

  4. Auto-renewing without reviewing. Plans change every year. Premiums go up, networks change, formularies shift. Review your plan every Open Enrollment — don't just click "renew."
