Do I Need a Medicare Agent, or Can I Enroll Myself?

The Health Experts Insurance · Medicare Basics

Let's start with the honest answer, even though we're an insurance agency: no, you don't need an agent. You can enroll in Medicare through Social Security, and you can compare and enroll in plans directly at Medicare.gov or by calling 1-800-MEDICARE. Plenty of people do it themselves every year. So the real question isn't "can I?" — it's "when does having help actually make a difference?" Let's look at both sides, sin vueltas.

Enrolling yourself: what it looks like

Medicare.gov has a Plan Finder tool that lets you enter your zip code, medications, and pharmacies, and compare the plans available in your area. If you're comfortable navigating websites, comparing formularies, and reading Evidence of Coverage documents, you can absolutely handle it. The DIY route works well when:

  • Your health situation is simple and stable
  • You take few or no prescriptions
  • You've done this before and know your enrollment windows
  • You genuinely enjoy reading plan documents (hey, some people do!)

Where people tend to get tripped up

In our experience helping South Florida families, the trouble usually isn't picking a plan — it's everything around the plan:

  • Enrollment timing. Missing your Initial Enrollment Period or going without creditable drug coverage can mean lifelong penalties. (We explain those on our Medicare penalties FAQ.)
  • Network surprises. A plan can look great on paper until you discover your cardiologist isn't in network — in a county like Miami-Dade with dozens of plan options, this is the #1 regret we hear.
  • Drug formulary details. The same medication can sit in different cost tiers on different plans.
  • Missed savings. Many people never check whether they qualify for a Medicare Savings Program or Extra Help — programs that can pay premiums for them.
  • The year-after problem. Plans change every year. Someone has to read that Annual Notice of Change.

What an agent does — and what one costs

Here's the part that surprises people: working with a licensed agent costs you nothing. Agents are paid by insurance carriers, at commission rates regulated by Medicare — and your premium is exactly the same whether you enroll through an agent, directly with the carrier, or on Medicare.gov. There's no markup for using one.

A good agent will verify your doctors and prescriptions against actual plan networks and formularies, walk you through enrollment windows, screen you for savings programs, handle the paperwork, and — the part clients value most — be a real person to call in March when a bill looks wrong. One important thing to understand: agents can only show you plans from the carriers they represent, which is why we're required to share the disclaimer at the bottom of this page. Transparency matters to us.

How to choose, honestly

If your situation is simple and you're comfortable online, self-enrolling is a perfectly valid choice. If you have multiple doctors, several prescriptions, a tight budget, or you'd just rather talk it through with a bilingual human who does this every day — that's what we're here for, and it doesn't cost you a penny either way. A good first step: answer a few questions in our Medicare plan finder so we understand your situation before we ever get on a call.

Frequently asked questions

Do I have to use an agent to enroll in Medicare?

No. You can enroll through Social Security and compare plans yourself at Medicare.gov or by calling 1-800-MEDICARE. An agent is optional.

How much does a Medicare agent cost?

Nothing. Agents are paid by carriers at Medicare-regulated rates, and your premium is the same either way.

What does a Medicare agent actually do?

Verifies your doctors and prescriptions against plan networks and formularies, explains enrollment windows and penalties, compares options from the carriers they represent, helps with applications, and provides year-round support.

Is it safe to use a Medicare agent?

Working with a licensed, certified agent is safe and regulated. Verify licensing, and remember: legitimate agents never pressure you and never claim to be calling from Medicare.
