Are Health Insurance Premiums Subject to FICA Taxes?

If you've ever looked at your pay stub and wondered why your health insurance deduction doesn't reduce your Social Security or Medicare tax, you're not alone. It's one of the most common payroll questions we hear — and the answer depends on how your premiums are set up.

The short version: most employer-sponsored health insurance premiums are exempt from FICA — but there are important exceptions, especially if you're self-employed, an S-corporation owner, or paying premiums after tax.

Here's everything you need to know.

What Is FICA?

FICA stands for the Federal Insurance Contributions Act. It's the payroll tax that funds Social Security and Medicare:

  • Social Security tax: 6.2% employee + 6.2% employer = 12.4% total
  • Medicare tax: 1.45% employee + 1.45% employer = 2.9% total
  • Total FICA: 7.65% from your paycheck, matched by your employer

FICA is separate from federal income tax. A deduction that reduces your income tax doesn't automatically reduce your FICA — and that's exactly what trips people up.

The General Rule: Employer-Paid Premiums Are FICA-Exempt

When your employer pays health insurance premiums on your behalf, those contributions are excluded from both income tax and FICA. They never appear on your W-2 at all. This is one of the most tax-efficient employee benefits available.

What About Your Payroll Deductions? It Depends on the Plan

If your employer offers health insurance and deducts your share from your paycheck, the tax treatment depends on whether you're enrolled in a Section 125 cafeteria plan.

Pre-Tax Through a Section 125 Cafeteria Plan (Most Common)

Most employer-sponsored health insurance uses a Section 125 cafeteria plan. When your premiums are deducted pre-tax under Section 125:

  • ✅ Exempt from federal income tax
  • ✅ Exempt from FICA (both Social Security and Medicare)
  • ✅ Exempt from most state income taxes

This is why your health insurance deduction reduces your taxable wages on your W-2, but your actual gross pay is higher. Your employer is saving on FICA too — roughly 7.65 cents per dollar of pre-tax premium.

How to confirm: Look at your pay stub. If your health insurance deduction is listed under "pre-tax deductions" or your W-2 Box 1 is lower than your actual gross earnings, you're in a Section 125 plan.

After-Tax Premiums (Less Common)

If your premiums are deducted after tax — meaning they come out after FICA is calculated — then:

  • ❌ Subject to FICA
  • ❌ Subject to federal income tax

After-tax health insurance is less common in traditional employer plans, but it happens — especially with certain voluntary benefits, COBRA continuation coverage, or plans that aren't set up as Section 125 cafeteria plans.

The silver lining: After-tax premiums can sometimes be deducted on your federal income tax return (Schedule A, if you itemize and your medical expenses exceed 7.5% of adjusted gross income). But you still pay FICA on them.

Self-Employed? The Rules Are Different

If you're self-employed — a sole proprietor, single-member LLC, or partnership — you pay self-employment tax instead of FICA. The rate is the same (15.3%), but you're covering both the employee and employer share.

Here's where it gets nuanced:

  • Health insurance premiums are deductible for income tax — you can deduct 100% of your premiums as an adjustment to income on your personal return (Schedule 1), which reduces your federal and state income tax.
  • But they are NOT deductible for self-employment tax. Your SE tax is calculated on your net self-employment income before the health insurance deduction. So you save on income tax, but not on the self-employment tax equivalent of FICA.

This is a significant distinction that surprises many self-employed people — and it's one reason why the structure of your business entity matters for health insurance costs.

S-Corporation Owners: A Special Case

If you own more than 2% of an S-corporation, the IRS treats you differently from regular employees.

  • Your S-corp can pay your health insurance premiums and deduct them as a business expense.
  • Those premiums must be included in your W-2 wages (Box 1) — meaning they're subject to income tax.
  • However, they are exempt from FICA (Social Security and Medicare taxes) as long as the premiums are properly set up under the S-corp's health insurance plan.
  • You can then deduct the premiums as a self-employed health insurance deduction on your personal return.

Getting this wrong is one of the most common S-corp payroll mistakes. If you're an S-corp owner, make sure your accountant is handling this correctly.

COBRA Premiums: Are They Subject to FICA?

If you're continuing coverage through COBRA after leaving a job, you're paying the full premium yourself — no employer contribution.

COBRA premiums are after-tax for most people, which means:

  • They're subject to FICA if you're still employed somewhere that withholds FICA on your income (COBRA premiums themselves aren't a payroll deduction — you pay them directly to the insurer or plan administrator)
  • They may be deductible as a medical expense on Schedule A if you itemize
  • They do not reduce your self-employment tax if you're self-employed

The bigger issue with COBRA is usually the cost — you pay 100% of the premium plus a 2% administrative fee, which often runs $600–$1,800/month for a family. Most people have better options.

What Are the Alternatives to COBRA?

If you've lost job-based coverage, COBRA isn't your only choice — and it's often not the best one. See our full COBRA vs. ACA Marketplace breakdown for a detailed comparison, but here's a quick overview:

  • ACA Marketplace plans: Losing job coverage triggers a 60-day Special Enrollment Period. Depending on your income, you may qualify for significant subsidies that make a Marketplace plan far cheaper than COBRA.
  • Private health insurance: Individual and family plans outside the Marketplace, available year-round in some cases.
  • Short-term health plans: Limited coverage, not ACA-compliant, but a lower-cost bridge option in some situations.

The Bottom Line

For most W-2 employees with employer-sponsored insurance through a Section 125 plan, health insurance premiums are not subject to FICA. The savings are automatic — you don't need to do anything to claim them.

Where it gets complicated is for the self-employed, S-corp owners, and anyone paying premiums outside of a formal cafeteria plan. If you're in one of those situations, the structure matters — and getting it right can save you meaningful money each year.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are employer health insurance premiums subject to FICA?

No. Employer-paid health insurance premiums are excluded from FICA taxes entirely. They don't appear on your W-2 and are not subject to Social Security or Medicare withholding.

Are my health insurance payroll deductions subject to FICA?

It depends. If your employer uses a Section 125 cafeteria plan — which most do — your pre-tax health insurance deductions are exempt from FICA. If your premiums are after-tax, they are subject to FICA.

Do self-employed people pay FICA on health insurance premiums?

Self-employed individuals pay self-employment tax (the equivalent of FICA) and cannot deduct health insurance premiums from their SE tax base. They can deduct premiums from income tax, but not self-employment tax.

Are S-corp owner health insurance premiums subject to FICA?

For owners with more than 2% of an S-corporation, health insurance premiums are included in W-2 wages for income tax purposes but are exempt from FICA if properly structured. Consult a tax professional to ensure correct setup.

Can I deduct COBRA premiums from FICA?

COBRA premiums are paid directly — they're not a payroll deduction. They may be deductible as a medical expense on Schedule A if you itemize, but they don't reduce FICA.
