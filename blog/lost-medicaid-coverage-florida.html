Lost Medicaid Coverage in Florida? Here's What to Do Next

Losing your Medicaid coverage is stressful — especially when it happens unexpectedly. If you recently got a notice saying your Florida Medicaid is ending or has already ended, you're not alone. Since the COVID-19 pandemic's continuous enrollment protections ended in 2023, Florida has disenrolled hundreds of thousands of residents from Medicaid. The process is ongoing, and it has left many people scrambling to find new coverage fast.

The good news? If you're on — or near — Medicare, you may have more options than you think. Let's walk through what you can do step by step.

Why Did You Lose Medicaid in Florida?

Florida Medicaid conducts annual eligibility redeterminations. If your income changed, your household size changed, or the state simply couldn't verify your information, you may have been disenrolled — even if you still qualify.

Common reasons Florida residents lose Medicaid:
- Income increased above the eligibility threshold
- Outdated contact information on file (notices went to the wrong address)
- Failure to return renewal paperwork in time
- Age — turning 65 and moving to Medicare territory

Whatever the reason, you have a window to act. Don't wait.

If You're 65+ or Have Medicare — You Have Special Enrollment Options

When you lose Medicaid, it triggers a Special Enrollment Period (SEP) for Medicare. This means you can enroll in or change Medicare Advantage or Part D (drug) plans outside of the standard October–December Open Enrollment window.

How Long Does the SEP Last?

When you lose Medicaid, you generally have 3 months from the date your Medicaid ends (or the date you're notified, whichever is later) to change your Medicare Advantage or Part D coverage without penalty.

Missing this window could mean waiting until the next Open Enrollment period or facing a coverage gap. Act quickly.

Medicare Savings Programs (MSPs): You May Still Qualify for Help

Even if you no longer qualify for full Medicaid, you may still be eligible for a Medicare Savings Program (MSP) — a lesser-known benefit that can dramatically reduce your Medicare costs.

Florida offers four MSP levels:

Program Who It Helps What It Covers
Qualified Medicare Beneficiary (QMB) Low income Pays Part A & B premiums, deductibles, co-pays
Specified Low-Income Medicare Beneficiary (SLMB) Slightly higher income Pays Part B premium only
Qualifying Individual (QI) A bit higher still Pays Part B premium (limited slots)
Qualified Disabled & Working Individual (QDWI) Disabled workers under 65 Pays Part A premium

Important: If you qualify for QMB, providers cannot bill you for cost-sharing. Zero out-of-pocket on Medicare-covered services. That's a powerful safety net.

To apply for an MSP in Florida, contact your local Department of Children and Families (DCF) office or visit ACCESS Florida online.

D-SNP Plans: The Bridge Between Medicaid and Medicare

If you have both Medicare and Medicaid (called "dual eligibility"), you may be eligible for a Dual Eligible Special Needs Plan (D-SNP) — a type of Medicare Advantage plan specifically designed for people in your situation.

D-SNPs can include:
- $0 or very low premiums
- Extra benefits like dental, vision, and hearing
- Care coordination to manage complex health needs
- Transportation to medical appointments
- Over-the-counter allowances for everyday health items

D-SNP eligibility in Florida generally requires that you have both Medicare Part A and Part B and that you qualify for some level of Medicaid — even a Medicare Savings Program can make you eligible.

This is an area where a licensed agent can make a real difference. The plan options vary by county across Florida, and what's available in Miami-Dade may differ from what's offered in Broward or Hillsborough.

What If You're Not Yet on Medicare?

If you're under 65 and lost Medicaid, you have different options:

  • ACA Marketplace plans: Losing Medicaid coverage triggers a Special Enrollment Period on HealthCare.gov. You have 60 days to enroll.
  • CHIP: If children in your household lost coverage, they may qualify for Florida KidCare.
  • Reapply for Medicaid: If your circumstances change again, you can reapply at any time.

Even if you think you make too much for Medicaid, you may qualify for subsidized Marketplace coverage at a very low monthly cost. Base ACA subsidies are still available depending on your income — the enhanced pandemic-era subsidies expired January 1, 2026, so it's worth getting a fresh quote to see what you now qualify for.

Steps to Take Right Now

  1. Find your Medicaid termination notice — it should include the reason and your appeal rights.
  2. Check if you qualify for Medicare — if you're 65+ or have been on Social Security Disability for 24 months.
  3. Look into Medicare Savings Programs — apply through DCF even if you're not sure you qualify.
  4. Ask about D-SNP plans — if you have or will have Medicare.
  5. Explore Marketplace coverage — if you're under 65 and your SEP window is open.
  6. Talk to a licensed agent — a real person who knows Florida's specific options can walk you through all of this without charging you a fee.
