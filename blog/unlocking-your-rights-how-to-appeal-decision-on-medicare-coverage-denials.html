Unlocking Your Rights: How to Appeal Decision on Medicare Coverage Denials

Published August 24, 2025

Receiving a notice that Medicare has denied coverage for a service, medication, or procedure can feel alarming — especially when your health is at stake. But here's what many beneficiaries don't know: a denial is not the final word. Medicare provides a formal, multi-step appeals process specifically designed to protect your rights. Understanding how to appeal a Medicare decision is one of the most valuable things you can do as a beneficiary.

Step One: Get the Denial in Writing

Before you can appeal anything, you need documentation. Always request and keep the written denial notice. Depending on your coverage type, this may arrive as:

  • An Explanation of Benefits (EOB) — sent after a claim is processed under Original Medicare (Parts A and B)
  • A Notice of Denial of Medical Coverage — issued by a Medicare Advantage or Part D plan when a service or drug is denied

These documents spell out exactly why the claim was denied and how to begin your appeal. Keep every piece of correspondence related to your care, including medical records, doctor's letters of medical necessity, and copies of any forms you submit. Documentation is your strongest tool throughout the appeals process.

The Five Levels of the Medicare Appeals Process

Medicare's appeals process is structured in five escalating levels. You must generally complete each level before moving to the next, although the time limits and requirements change at each stage.

Level 1: Redetermination

The first step is filing a Redetermination — a request for the same entity that made the original decision to take a second look. For Original Medicare claims, you submit this to the Medicare Administrative Contractor (MAC) that processed the claim. For Medicare Advantage or Part D plans, you submit directly to your plan. You have 120 days from the date you receive the denial to file, but acting within 60 days is strongly recommended to protect your rights at subsequent levels. A decision is typically issued within 60 days.

Level 2: Reconsideration by a Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC)

If you're unsatisfied with the Redetermination outcome, you can escalate to a Reconsideration review. For Original Medicare, this is handled by a Qualified Independent Contractor (QIC). For Medicare Advantage and Part D, the independent reviewer is the Independent Review Entity (IRE), currently administered by MAXIMUS Federal Services. You generally have 60 days from receipt of the Level 1 decision to file. This review is conducted by an organization that is completely independent from Medicare and the insurance plan, providing a genuinely fresh perspective on your case.

Level 3: Administrative Law Judge (ALJ) Hearing

If the QIC or IRE upholds the denial, you may request a hearing before an Administrative Law Judge (ALJ). There is an important threshold at this level: the amount in controversy must be at least $180 (this figure is adjusted annually). The ALJ hearing gives you the opportunity to present your case in person, by phone, or by video. You may also bring witnesses or have a representative speak on your behalf. This level often produces favorable outcomes for beneficiaries who come well-prepared with supporting medical documentation.

Level 4: Medicare Appeals Council

If the ALJ rules against you, the next step is requesting a review by the Medicare Appeals Council (also called the Departmental Appeals Board). You have 60 days from the ALJ decision to file this request. The Appeals Council reviews the ALJ's decision for legal and procedural errors. While they may issue their own ruling, they may also send the case back to the ALJ for further review.

Level 5: Federal District Court

The final level of appeal is filing a lawsuit in a U.S. Federal District Court. This option is available if all four prior levels have been exhausted and a minimum amount-in-controversy threshold is met (adjusted annually). This level typically requires an attorney. While federal court review is relatively rare, it represents the ultimate safeguard built into the system to protect Medicare beneficiaries.

Special Rules for Medicare Advantage Plans

If you are enrolled in a Medicare Advantage (Part C) plan, the appeals process has a slightly different starting point. You must first go through your plan's internal appeal process before escalating to the Independent Review Entity (IRE). Most plans have specific forms and deadlines for internal appeals, which are outlined in your plan's Evidence of Coverage (EOC) document. Make sure you know your plan's procedures — missing a deadline can complicate your case.

Expedited Appeals for Urgent Care Situations

When your health is in immediate jeopardy, you don't have to wait through the standard timeline. Medicare offers an expedited (fast-track) appeal process for urgent situations. Under this process, a decision must be issued within 72 hours. To qualify, your doctor must confirm — or you can assert on your own — that waiting for a standard appeal decision would seriously harm your health. Expedited appeals are available at the Redetermination and Reconsideration levels.

Free Help Is Available: SHIP Counselors

You don't have to navigate the appeals process alone. Every state has a State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP), which provides free, unbiased counseling to Medicare beneficiaries. SHIP counselors can help you:

  • Understand why a claim was denied
  • Gather supporting documentation from your doctor
  • Complete appeal forms correctly
  • Meet filing deadlines
  • Prepare for ALJ hearings

You can also call 1-800-MEDICARE (1-800-633-4227), available 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, for guidance on your rights and the appeals process. TTY users can call 1-877-486-2048.

Tips for a Stronger Appeal

  • Act quickly. Deadlines at each level are firm. Missing them can cost you your right to appeal.
  • Get a letter of medical necessity. A statement from your treating physician explaining why the service is medically necessary is often the most powerful piece of evidence you can submit.
  • Keep copies of everything. Send all correspondence via certified mail and keep records of every call, form, and letter.
  • Reference Medicare's coverage policies. If Medicare has a published coverage determination for the service in question, cite it. Your doctor's treatment aligning with established clinical guidelines strengthens your case.
  • Don't give up at Level 1. Statistics show that many denials are overturned when beneficiaries persist through the appeals process. Your persistence matters.

You Have Rights — Exercise Them

A Medicare coverage denial can feel like a dead end, but the appeals process exists precisely because Congress recognized that mistakes happen and that beneficiaries deserve a fair hearing. Whether you are dealing with Original Medicare or a Medicare Advantage plan, you have the legal right to challenge any decision that affects your care.

Understanding the five levels of appeal, knowing the deadlines, gathering strong medical evidence, and taking advantage of free counseling resources like SHIP can make a real difference in the outcome of your case.
