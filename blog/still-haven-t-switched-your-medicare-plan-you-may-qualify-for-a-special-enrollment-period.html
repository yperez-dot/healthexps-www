Still Haven’t Switched Your Medicare Plan? You May Qualify for a Special Enrollment Period

Published December 12, 2025

If your Medicare plan ended on December 31, 2025 — including AvMed Medicare — and you have not yet made a new plan selection, you may be wondering what your options are now that the Annual Enrollment Period (AEP) has long since closed. The good news: you may still be able to enroll in a new Medicare plan today, thanks to a Special Enrollment Period (SEP) that is triggered when your plan is involuntarily terminated.

This article explains what an SEP is, who qualifies, what your coverage looks like if you have not yet acted, and what steps you can take right now to protect yourself.

What Is a Special Enrollment Period?

Medicare's Annual Enrollment Period runs from October 15 to December 7 each year. During that window, beneficiaries can join, switch, or drop Medicare Advantage plans and Part D prescription drug plans. But life does not always follow a calendar. That is why Medicare provides Special Enrollment Periods — time-limited windows outside of AEP that allow you to make coverage changes when a qualifying life event occurs.

A plan termination — meaning your insurance carrier decides to stop offering a Medicare plan in your area — is one of the most common qualifying events for an SEP. When a plan is discontinued, every affected member automatically qualifies for this special window to choose a new plan.

AvMed Medicare Ended December 31, 2025

In late 2025, AvMed notified its Medicare members that it would be discontinuing its Medicare Advantage plans in Florida effective December 31, 2025. Tens of thousands of beneficiaries across South Florida received termination notices, giving them a limited window during AEP to find a replacement plan.

However, not everyone acted in time. Some people were confused by the notices. Others assumed their coverage would automatically transfer. And some simply did not know where to start. If that describes you, read on — because you likely still have options.

How Long Does the Plan Termination SEP Last?

When a Medicare plan is terminated, affected members typically receive a Special Enrollment Period that lasts for two months after the plan ends. For plans that ended December 31, 2025, that standard SEP window would have covered January and February 2026.

If you are reading this and that window has already passed, do not stop reading. There are additional SEPs that may still apply to you — particularly if you live in Florida.

Florida Hurricane Milton SEP: Open Through July 31, 2026

In the aftermath of Hurricane Milton, the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) granted a special disaster-related SEP for Florida beneficiaries. This SEP allows eligible Florida residents to make changes to their Medicare Advantage or Part D coverage even outside the standard enrollment windows.

The Hurricane Milton SEP is currently active and runs through July 31, 2026.

This means that if you are a Florida Medicare beneficiary who has not yet enrolled in a new plan after your AvMed coverage ended, the Hurricane Milton SEP may give you the window you need to make a change right now — before you lose this opportunity entirely.

What Happens If You Have Not Switched?

If your Medicare Advantage plan was terminated and you did not enroll in a new plan, you were automatically returned to Original Medicare (Parts A and B). While Original Medicare provides hospital and medical coverage, there are two significant gaps to be aware of:

  • No prescription drug coverage (Part D): Original Medicare does not cover most outpatient prescription drugs. If you were receiving drug coverage through your former Medicare Advantage plan, you may currently have no drug coverage at all.
  • No out-of-pocket maximum: Unlike Medicare Advantage plans, Original Medicare has no annual cap on your out-of-pocket costs. A serious illness or hospital stay can result in very significant expenses.
  • No extra benefits: Many Medicare Advantage plans include dental, vision, hearing, and fitness benefits. Original Medicare does not cover these services.

This is why taking action now — while an SEP may still be available — is so important.

Your Coverage Options

If you qualify for an SEP, you generally have the following paths to consider. A licensed broker can walk you through each one in detail and help you compare based on your doctors, medications, and budget.

Medicare Advantage (Part C)

Medicare Advantage plans are offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare and replace Original Medicare. Many plans bundle prescription drug coverage along with extra benefits such as dental, vision, and hearing — often at low or no additional monthly premium. Plan availability, benefits, and costs vary by carrier and county.

Medicare Supplement (Medigap) + Part D

If you prefer the flexibility of Original Medicare — seeing any doctor who accepts Medicare, anywhere in the country — a Medicare Supplement plan (also called Medigap) can help cover the out-of-pocket costs that Original Medicare does not pay. You would pair this with a standalone Part D prescription drug plan to cover your medications. This combination typically comes with a higher monthly premium but offers broader provider access.

There is no single right answer. The best choice depends on your individual health needs, your budget, and the providers you rely on. An independent broker can compare your options side by side at no cost to you.

Special Note: Dual-Eligible Beneficiaries

If you are enrolled in both Medicare and Medicaid — sometimes called being “dual eligible” or receiving the Extra Help / Low Income Subsidy (LIS) program — you have even more flexibility than other Medicare beneficiaries. Dual-eligible individuals can generally switch Medicare Advantage or Part D plans at any time throughout the year, not just during AEP or a special SEP window. If this applies to you, you do not need to wait for any deadline. Contact a broker today to review your options.

Don’t Wait — the July 31, 2026 Deadline Is Approaching

The Hurricane Milton SEP ends July 31, 2026. After that date, most beneficiaries who have not yet made a plan selection will need to wait until the next Annual Enrollment Period — October 15 through December 7, 2026 — before they can enroll in a new plan. That means potentially months of continuing with no drug coverage, no dental or vision benefits, and no out-of-pocket maximum protection.
