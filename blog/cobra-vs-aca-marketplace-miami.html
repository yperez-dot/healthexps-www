COBRA vs ACA Marketplace in Miami: Which One Actually Saves You More Money?

Published June 24, 2026 · Category: Private Insurance · Voice: Katy

You just lost your job-based coverage. The COBRA letter arrives. The number is shocking: $850/month for the same plan you had when your employer was paying most of it.

Before you panic-accept COBRA, you need to know this: most Miami residents save $400-$600/month by switching to an ACA Marketplace plan instead — with the same or better coverage.

Here's exactly how the two options compare, what each one actually costs in Florida, and which one saves you more money.

What COBRA Actually Costs

COBRA (Consolidated Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act) lets you keep your former employer's health plan after you lose your job. Sounds great — except for one detail: you now pay 100% of the premium your employer was paying, plus a 2% administrative fee.

For most people in Florida, that means:

  • $800–$1,020/month for a single person
  • $2,000–$2,800/month for families

If you were paying $150/month when your employer covered 80%, that same plan now costs $850/month. That's a 5x increase for the exact same coverage.

What Is the ACA Marketplace?

The ACA Marketplace (also called Obamacare or HealthCare.gov) is a federal and state exchange where you can buy private health insurance. Unlike COBRA, ACA plans come with income-based subsidies that can dramatically reduce your monthly premium — in many cases down to $0–$150/month for comprehensive coverage.

When you lose job-based coverage, it triggers a 60-day Special Enrollment Period that lets you sign up for an ACA plan even outside of Open Enrollment.

Side-by-Side Comparison

COBRA ACA Marketplace
Monthly cost (single) $800–$1,020 $0–$350 (after subsidies)
Monthly cost (family) $2,000–$2,800 $0–$1,200 (after subsidies)
Who qualifies Anyone who had employer coverage Anyone with a qualifying life event (job loss counts)
Coverage period Up to 18 months 12 months (renews annually)
Provider networks Same as your old employer plan Varies by carrier (Florida Blue, Ambetter, Oscar, etc.)
Prescription coverage Same as your old plan Varies by plan tier (Bronze/Silver/Gold)
When to enroll Within 60 days of losing coverage Within 60 days of losing coverage (Special Enrollment Period)

Who Should Keep COBRA

COBRA isn't always the wrong choice. You should consider keeping COBRA if:

  • You're in active cancer treatment and need to keep your current oncologist and treatment facility.
  • You're pregnant and want to deliver with the same doctor and hospital you've been seeing.
  • You're mid-treatment with a very specific specialist who isn't in any ACA plan networks.
  • You need bridge coverage for a short gap (e.g., 2-3 months before Medicare eligibility, a new job, or moving out of state).

In these cases, the higher cost may be worth keeping continuity of care.

Who Should Switch to ACA

Most people save money by switching to an ACA plan — especially if:

  • You're healthy or have manageable chronic conditions (diabetes, high blood pressure, etc.).
  • Your income qualifies for subsidies (most people who just lost a job qualify).
  • You don't need a specific specialist or your doctors accept ACA plans.
  • You want to save $400-$600/month without sacrificing comprehensive coverage.

ACA plans cover all 10 essential health benefits — the same coverage categories as COBRA.

The 60-Day Window

Whether you choose COBRA or ACA, you have 60 days from losing coverage to make a decision.

  • COBRA: You have 60 days to elect COBRA coverage. If you wait longer, you lose the option.
  • ACA: Losing job coverage triggers a 60-day Special Enrollment Period. You must enroll within 60 days or wait until the next Open Enrollment (November 1 – January 15).

Pro tip: You don't have to decide on Day 1. You can take a few weeks to compare ACA options before your 60-day window closes.

Real Miami Example

Let's say you're a single 45-year-old in Miami who just lost your job. Your income for this year will be around $50,000 (from the months you were employed).

  • COBRA: $820/month for your old employer plan.
  • ACA Silver plan: $150/month after subsidies (comprehensive coverage, $3,500 deductible, includes prescription coverage).

Savings by switching to ACA: $670/month = $8,040/year.

That's real money — enough to cover rent, groceries, or building an emergency fund while you job search.

FAQ

Can I switch from COBRA to ACA mid-year?

Yes — but only within your 60-day Special Enrollment Period after losing job coverage. Once that window closes, you're locked into COBRA until the next ACA Open Enrollment (November 1 – January 15).

What if I get a new job while on ACA?

If your new employer offers group health insurance, you can drop your ACA plan and switch to the employer plan during your new employer's open enrollment or waiting period. You won't owe penalties for dropping ACA mid-year due to gaining new employer coverage.

Do I have to choose COBRA first?

No. You can skip COBRA entirely and go straight to an ACA plan. Many people assume they have to elect COBRA before exploring other options — that's not true. You can compare ACA plans, enroll, and never activate COBRA.

The Bottom Line

For most Miami residents, ACA Marketplace plans save $400-$600/month compared to COBRA — with the same comprehensive coverage, prescription benefits, and preventive care.

COBRA makes sense if you're mid-treatment with a specialist you can't lose. For everyone else, ACA is almost always the smarter financial choice.

You have 60 days to decide. Use them wisely.
