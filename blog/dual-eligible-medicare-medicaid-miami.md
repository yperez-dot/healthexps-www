# Dual Eligible: What It Means to Have Medicare and Medicaid Together in Miami

If you have Medicare **and** Florida Medicaid — or you think you might qualify for both — you're what's called **dual eligible**. In Miami-Dade, that's a big deal: dual-eligible residents have access to special plans, extra benefits, and prescription savings that most people on Medicare never see.

It's also one of the most under-used opportunities we come across. Every month, clients walk into our Doral office paying costs that Medicaid or a Medicare Savings Program could be covering.

## How Medicare and Medicaid work together

Think of it this way: **Medicare pays first, Medicaid fills in behind it.** Medicare (a federal program based on age or disability) covers your hospital and medical care. Medicaid (a state program based on income) can pick up costs Medicare leaves behind — premiums, deductibles, copays — and can cover services Medicare doesn't, like long-term care.

There are two levels of dual eligibility:

- **Full dual eligible:** you qualify for full Florida Medicaid benefits alongside Medicare.
- **Partial dual eligible:** your income is a little too high for full Medicaid, but you qualify for a **Medicare Savings Program (MSP)** that pays some of your Medicare costs.

## The Medicare Savings Programs: Florida can pay your Part B premium

The MSPs are the hidden gem here. Depending on your income level:

- **QMB (Qualified Medicare Beneficiary):** pays your Part B premium *and* your Medicare deductibles and copays. Providers are not allowed to bill QMB members for Medicare cost-sharing — that protection is federal law.
- **SLMB and QI:** pay your monthly Part B premium ($202.90 in 2026 — that's over $2,400 a year back in your pocket).

Income limits adjust every year and many people assume they earn too much when they don't — Social Security income is counted differently than most people expect. Our [Medicare Savings Program guide](/medicare-savings-program) covers the details, or call us and we'll screen you in five minutes.

## D-SNPs: Medicare Advantage plans built for dual eligibles

Miami-Dade has one of the largest selections of **Dual Eligible Special Needs Plans (D-SNPs)** in the country. These are Medicare Advantage plans designed specifically for people with both Medicare and Medicaid, and many include benefits regular plans don't, such as:

- Monthly allowances for over-the-counter items, and in many plans, healthy food
- Expanded dental, vision, and hearing coverage
- Transportation to medical appointments
- Care coordinators who help manage both programs together

Benefits vary significantly by plan and by county, and not every plan fits every situation — the right D-SNP depends on your doctors, your medications, and your level of Medicaid. ## Extra Help: prescription costs, cut down automatically

If you're dual eligible, you automatically qualify for **Extra Help** (the Low-Income Subsidy) with Part D prescription costs. That means reduced or eliminated drug plan premiums and deductibles, and low fixed copays on covered medications — a benefit worth thousands of dollars a year for many beneficiaries.

If you're *not* dual eligible but your income is limited, you may still qualify for Extra Help on its own. It's a separate application through Social Security, and yes — we help with it, at no charge.

## You can change plans more often than everyone else

Most people on Medicare are locked into their plan except during the fall Annual Enrollment Period. Dual-eligible and Extra Help beneficiaries have **monthly opportunities** to make certain changes — including enrolling in an integrated D-SNP or moving to Original Medicare with a standalone drug plan. If your plan isn't working — a doctor left the network, a medication got expensive — you usually don't have to wait until October. ([Here's our full guide to enrollment periods.](/blog/medicare-enrollment-periods-explained/))

## Common Miami situations we see every week

- **"I have Medicare but I lost my Medicaid at renewal."** Florida re-checks Medicaid eligibility. If you were dropped, you may still qualify for an MSP — and losing Medicaid triggers a Special Enrollment Period to change your Medicare plan.
- **"My mom has both but she's paying copays at the pharmacy."** She may not be in a plan that coordinates with her Medicaid level, or her Extra Help isn't applied. Fixable.
- **"I was told I make too much for Medicaid."** For *full* Medicaid, maybe. For an MSP, the limits are higher than most people think. Get screened before you assume.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What does "dual eligible" mean?**
It means you qualify for both Medicare and Medicaid at the same time. Medicare pays for your care first, and Medicaid helps cover remaining costs like premiums, deductibles, and copays, depending on your eligibility level.

**What is a D-SNP plan?**
A Dual Eligible Special Needs Plan is a type of Medicare Advantage plan designed for people with both Medicare and Medicaid. Many D-SNPs in Miami-Dade include extra benefits such as OTC allowances, dental, vision, and transportation.

**Can Florida pay my Medicare Part B premium?**
Yes — if you qualify for a Medicare Savings Program such as QMB, SLMB, or QI, the state pays your Part B premium ($202.90/month in 2026). QMB also covers Medicare deductibles and copays.

**Do dual-eligible beneficiaries get help with prescriptions?**
Yes. Dual-eligible individuals automatically qualify for Extra Help (the Low-Income Subsidy), which lowers or eliminates Part D premiums and deductibles and caps copays on covered drugs.

**Can I change my plan outside the fall enrollment period if I'm dual eligible?**
In most cases, yes. Dual-eligible and Extra Help beneficiaries have monthly opportunities to make certain plan changes, rather than waiting for the October 15 – December 7 Annual Enrollment Period.
