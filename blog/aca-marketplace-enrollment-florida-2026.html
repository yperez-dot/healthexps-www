When Can I Enroll in ACA Marketplace Plans in Florida? (2026 Guide)

Published June 25, 2026 | Health Experts

If you're shopping for health insurance in Florida outside of an employer plan, the ACA Marketplace is one of the most important tools available to you. But understanding when you can enroll — and what determines your eligibility — can be confusing.

This guide covers everything you need to know about ACA Marketplace enrollment in Florida for 2026, including Open Enrollment windows, Special Enrollment Periods, and what actually triggers your eligibility to sign up year-round.

What Is the ACA Marketplace?

The ACA Marketplace (also called the Health Insurance Exchange, or Healthcare.gov) is where individuals and families who don't have employer-sponsored coverage can shop for health insurance plans that comply with the Affordable Care Act.

All Marketplace plans must:

  • Cover 10 essential health benefits (including preventive care, emergency services, mental health, and prescription drugs)
  • Accept applicants regardless of pre-existing conditions
  • Offer subsidies (Premium Tax Credits) to eligible enrollees

Florida uses the federal Marketplace at healthcare.gov, not a state-run exchange.

Open Enrollment: When Is It?

For 2026 coverage, Open Enrollment ran from November 1 to January 15, 2026. This is the standard annual window when anyone can enroll in or change a Marketplace plan, regardless of their current coverage situation.

For 2027 plans, Open Enrollment will run November 1, 2026 – January 15, 2027. If you want coverage starting January 1, 2027, you must enroll by December 15, 2026.

Special Enrollment Periods: Year-Round Eligibility

Outside of Open Enrollment, you can still enroll in a Marketplace plan if you experience a qualifying life event — called a Special Enrollment Period (SEP). Once triggered, you typically have 60 days to enroll.

The most common qualifying life events in Florida include:

Losing Coverage

  • Losing job-based coverage (including COBRA ending)
  • Aging off a parent's plan at 26
  • Losing Medicaid eligibility
  • Coverage ending due to divorce or death of policyholder

Household Changes

  • Getting married or entering a domestic partnership
  • Having a baby, adopting, or taking in a foster child
  • Getting divorced (if it results in loss of coverage)

Moving

  • Moving to a new zip code or county (even within Florida)
  • Moving to Florida from another state
  • Students moving away from home

Income Changes

  • Significant change in household income that affects subsidy eligibility
  • Gaining or losing a dependent

How Income Affects Your ACA Plan Cost in Florida

One of the most important factors in your ACA plan cost is your household income relative to the Federal Poverty Level (FPL). Here's how it works for 2026:

  • Under 100% FPL (roughly $15,060 for a single person): May qualify for Medicaid (Florida has strict eligibility)
  • 100%–400% FPL ($15,060–$60,240 for a single person): Eligible for Premium Tax Credits
  • Above 400% FPL: May still qualify for credits under the enhanced ARP provisions extended through 2025–2026

For many Miami residents, this means paying far less than the "sticker price" for health coverage. A 35-year-old earning $35,000/year might pay as little as $50–$100/month for a Silver plan after credits are applied.

Marketplace Plan Tiers in Florida

Marketplace plans in Florida are offered in four metal tiers:

  • Bronze: Lowest monthly premium, highest deductible. Good if you're healthy and rarely use care.
  • Silver: Mid-range premium and deductible. The only tier eligible for Cost-Sharing Reductions (additional savings for lower-income enrollees).
  • Gold: Higher premium, lower deductible. Good if you use healthcare regularly.
  • Platinum: Highest premium, lowest out-of-pocket costs. Best for people with frequent or high-cost healthcare needs.

If you qualify for Cost-Sharing Reductions (income below 250% FPL), enrolling in a Silver plan is almost always the best financial decision — even if a Bronze plan has a lower premium.

What to Do Before Enrolling

Before you jump into a plan, there are a few things worth confirming:

  1. Check your doctors — Not all Florida plans include the same hospital networks. Verify that your preferred doctors and hospitals are in-network for any plan you're considering.
  2. Review your prescriptions — Each plan has a formulary. If you take regular medications, make sure they're covered before enrolling.
  3. Estimate your income accurately — Your subsidy is calculated based on your projected annual income. If you over-estimate, you may owe money back at tax time. If you under-estimate, you'll receive a credit.
  4. Don't auto-renew without comparing — Plan benefits, premiums, and networks change every year. What was the best plan last year may not be this year.
