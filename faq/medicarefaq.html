📘 Medicare Basics
Medicare has four parts:

Part A — Hospital insurance. Covers inpatient care, skilled nursing, hospice, and some home health. Free for most people.
Part B — Medical insurance. Covers doctor visits, outpatient care, preventive services, and medical equipment. $185/month in 2026.
Part C — Medicare Advantage. A private plan alternative that bundles A + B, usually with extras like dental and vision.
Part D — Prescription drugs. Available standalone or included in most Advantage plans.

Full guide to Medicare parts →
You qualify for Medicare if you are: a U.S. citizen or permanent resident who has lived in the U.S. for at least 5 continuous years, AND you meet one of these: (1) Age 65 or older, (2) Under 65 with a qualifying disability after receiving SSDI for 24 months, or (3) Any age with End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) or ALS (Lou Gehrig's disease).
You can enroll in Medicare three ways: (1) Online at ssa.gov, (2) By calling Social Security at 1-800-772-1213, or (3) In person at your local Social Security office. Most people should enroll during their Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) — a 7-month window around their 65th birthday. See enrollment deadlines →
Medicare is a federal health insurance program primarily for people 65 and older, regardless of income. Medicaid is a joint federal-state program that provides health coverage for people with limited income and resources, at any age. Some people qualify for both — they're called "dual-eligible" beneficiaries and may qualify for special plans with extra benefits and $0 copays.
🏥 Part A — Hospital Insurance
Part A covers: Inpatient hospital care (semi-private room, meals, nursing, medications), Skilled nursing facility care (up to 100 days after a qualifying 3-night hospital stay), Hospice care (for terminal illness), and Some home health care (skilled nursing or therapy, if homebound). Part A does NOT cover custodial care (help with daily activities).
Premium: Most people pay $0 — if you or your spouse worked and paid Medicare taxes for at least 10 years (40 quarters). If you have fewer quarters, you may pay a premium.

👨‍⚕️ Part B — Medical Insurance
Part B covers two types of services: Medically necessary services (doctor visits, outpatient care, ambulance, mental health, durable medical equipment, some home health) and Preventive services (flu shots, mammograms, colonoscopies, annual wellness visits — usually at no cost). Part B does NOT cover dental, vision, hearing, or prescription drugs.
The standard Part B premium is $185/month in 2026. You also pay a $257 annual deductible, then 20% of Medicare-approved costs for most services. There is no annual out-of-pocket cap on Original Medicare Part B — which is why many people add a Medigap plan or choose Medicare Advantage. Higher-income individuals pay more due to IRMAA surcharges. See full cost breakdown →
If a doctor doesn't accept Medicare assignment, they can charge up to 15% more than Medicare's approved amount — this extra amount is called a Part B excess charge. You're responsible for paying it unless you have a Medigap Plan G or Plan F, which cover excess charges. Most doctors in Florida accept Medicare assignment, but it's worth confirming before an appointment.
If you didn't enroll in Part B when first eligible and don't have qualifying employer coverage, you'll pay a 10% penalty added to your premium for each 12-month period you were eligible but didn't enroll. This penalty is permanent and added for as long as you have Part B. It's one of the most costly Medicare mistakes — avoid it by enrolling on time. Enrollment deadlines →
⭐ Part C — Medicare Advantage
Medicare Advantage (Part C) is an alternative to Original Medicare offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare. Instead of getting your benefits directly from the government, a private insurer manages your coverage. Plans must cover everything Original Medicare covers, but they can add extra benefits — dental, vision, hearing, OTC allowances, transportation, and more. You still pay your Part B premium ($185/month). Full Medicare Advantage guide →
By law, all Medicare Advantage plans must have an annual out-of-pocket maximum — in 2026, the maximum is $9,350 in-network. Once you hit this limit, the plan pays 100% of covered services for the rest of the year. This is one of the biggest advantages over Original Medicare, which has no out-of-pocket cap.
HMO (Health Maintenance Organization): You choose a primary care doctor who coordinates your care. You need referrals to see specialists. You must use in-network providers except in emergencies. Generally the lowest premiums and copays.

PPO (Preferred Provider Organization): No referrals needed. You can see out-of-network providers but pay more. More flexibility and a wider network. Usually slightly higher premiums than HMOs.
It depends on the specific plan. Not all Medicare Advantage plans include all doctors in their network. Before enrolling in any plan, you should verify that your specific doctors and specialists are covered. Our brokers check network participation for every doctor you name — before recommending a plan. This is the most important step and is completely free.
Some Medicare Advantage plans in Florida offer a give-back benefit — they pay part of your Part B premium for you, reducing your monthly Social Security deduction. The amount varies by plan, ranging from $10 to the full $185/month. Ask us which plans offer this in your ZIP code.
💊 Part D — Prescription Drugs
Part D covers prescription drugs. Each plan has a formulary — a list of covered drugs organized into tiers. Lower-tier drugs (generics) cost less; higher-tier specialty drugs cost more. In 2026, Part D has a $2,000 out-of-pocket cap — after that, your plan pays 100% of drug costs for the rest of the year. This is new as of 2025 and significantly limits your maximum drug exposure.

If you don't enroll in Part D when first eligible and don't have other creditable drug coverage, you'll pay a penalty of 1% of the national base beneficiary premium for each month you were without coverage. This penalty is added to your Part D premium for as long as you have Medicare drug coverage. It's permanent — don't delay enrolling if you take any medications.
Extra Help (also called Low Income Subsidy or LIS) is a federal program that helps people with limited income pay for Part D costs. It can reduce or eliminate your Part D premium, deductible, and copays. In 2026, individuals earning up to approximately $1,903/month may qualify. If you qualify for QMB, SLMB, or QI through the Medicare Savings Program, you automatically qualify for Extra Help. Check your eligibility →
🛡️ Medicare Supplement (Medigap)
A Medigap plan is private insurance that works alongside Original Medicare to cover the costs Medicare doesn't pay — the 20% coinsurance, Part A deductible, hospital copays, and more. Plan G is the most comprehensive option for new enrollees. With Plan G, once you pay the $257 Part B deductible, Medicare and Medigap together cover nearly everything. You can see any doctor nationwide that accepts Medicare. Compare plan letters →
The best time is during your 6-month Medigap Open Enrollment Period, which begins the month you turn 65 AND are enrolled in Part B. During this window, insurers cannot deny you coverage or charge you more based on pre-existing conditions. After this window, you may face medical underwriting and could be denied or charged significantly more based on your health history.
Plan G premiums in Florida typically range from $100–$220/month for a 65-year-old, depending on gender, tobacco use, and the insurance carrier. Because all Plan G policies cover exactly the same things regardless of carrier, the only difference is price. Premiums for identical coverage can vary by $50–$100/month between companies in the same ZIP code. Our brokers compare every carrier at no cost. Use the premium estimator →
